1) Which of the following chemicals are amphoteric?
A) HCl
B) LiOH
C) PO43-
D) HSO4-*
Amphoteric (amph- like amphibian, means both) means that it can act as either an acid or a base.  What you should be looking for is a chemical with an acidic hydrogen (typically it will have the hydrogen listed first or a carboxylic acid group -COOH), that can also accept an additional hydrogen (e.g. water or negatively charged).  Thus HSO4- can be classified as amphoteric.

2) What is the conjugate base of  H2O?
A) H3O+
B) OH-*
C) O2-
D) None of the above.
To get the conjugate base, you'd need to remove a H+ or add an OH-.  In this case, when we remove a H+ from H2O it gives us O2-.

3) Which of the following Ka values would correspond to the strongest acid?
A) Ka = 2.40 x 10-2*
B) Ka = 6.39 x 10-9
C) Ka = 8.89 x 10-4
D) Ka = 1.07 x 10-5
Since Ka is essentially products over reactants, the highest Ka corresponds with the strongest acid.

4) Determine the pH in a 0.278 M H3PO4 solution. The Ka of H3PO4 is 7.5 × 10-3.
A) 1.34
B) 3.35
C) 1.38*
D) None of the above.
Since H3PO4 is a weak acid, we need a RICE table, and acid dissociation constant equation.
R H3PO4 ⇄ H+ +  H2PO4-
I 0.278 0 0
C - x + x + x
E 0.278 - x x x

Ka = 7.5 × 10-3 = [H+][H2PO4-]/[H3PO4]
7.5 × 10-3= x2/(0.278 - x), small x assumption is not valid because 0.278/(7.5 × 10-3) = 37.1
7.5 × 10-3 = x2/0.278
(0.278 - x) x (7.5 × 10-3) = x2
0.00209 - 7.5 × 10-3x = x2
0 = x2 + 7.5 × 10-3x - 0.00209
x = 0.0421
pH = -log[H3O+]
pH = -log(0.0421)
pH = 1.38

12) Which of the following is the strongest Lewis acid (best at giving up H+ or binding OH-)?
A) Na+
B) K+
C) Li+
D) HF*
The strongest acid, will have the weakest conjugate base.  The conjugate bases of Na+, Li+, and K+ are all strong bases (NaOH, LiOH, and KOH).  Thus the answer is HF.

25) (SLO 4.2) Which of the following is a Brønsted–Lowry base?
A) HClO2
B) CHCH
C) Cl2
D) LiOH*
E) None of the above.
HClO2 is an acid.

28) What would be the pH of a solution formed by mixing 300 mL of 1.57 M NH4Cl with 300 mL of 0.885 M NH3?  The Ka for NH4+ is 6.3x10-10.
A) 9.20
B) 7.93
C) 9.45
D) 8.95*
In this case, you're mixing a weak acid with it's weak conjugate base.
This will create a buffer solution, for which we will use the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation to solve for pH.
First we need the moles of the acid and the base.
[NH4Cl] = 3 mL x (1 L/1000 mL) x (1.57 mol/1L) = 0.00471 mol
[NH3] = 3 mL x (1 L/1000 mL) x (0.885 mol/1L) = 0.00266 mol*
pH = -log(Ka) + log(base/acid) = -log(6.3x10-10) + log(0.00266/0.00471)
pH = 8.95
*since we were using the same volume, we could have just plugged in molarity (before mixing) as a short-cut, or we could have calculated millimoles by not dividing by 1000, as a different shortcut.

30) You add 600 mL of a 0.150 M NaOH solution to 600 mL of a 0.150 M HClO solution (Ka for HClO is 3.5x10-8).  What will be the pH of that solution?
A) 3.76
B) 10.2*

0.0900 mol of NaOH will react with 0.0900 mol of HClO to produce 0.0900 mol of ClO-.

0.0900 mol / 1.20 L = 0.0750 M
R  ClO- + H2O ⇄ HClO +  OH-
I 0.0750   0 0
C - x   + x + x
E 0.0750 - x   x x

Kb = 10-14/3.5x10-8 = 2.86x10-7
Kb = 2.86x10-7 = x2/(0.0750 - x), the small x assumption is valid because 0.0750/2.86x10-7 = 2.63x105
2.86x10-7 = x2/(0.0750)

(0.0750) x 2.86x10-7 = x2

31) If you are using an acid and it's conjugate base to prepare a buffer that will be effective at a pH of 7.00 at 25°C, which of the following Ka values would be best for your acid?
A) 1.02x10-7*
B) 1.10x10-6
C) 1.03x10-8
D) 9.94x10-10
You want -log(Ka) to be as close to your desired pH as possible, because then the buffer solution will have an equal amount of acid and conjugate base (the 1/2 equivalence point).
- log(1.02x10-7) = 6.99, which is the closest to the pH of 7.

32) What would be the pH of a solution formed by mixing 600 mL of 0.878 M HC2H3O2 with 600 mL of 1.65 M NaC2H3O2?  The Kb for C2H3O2- is 5.71x10-10.
A) 5.03*
B) 4.48
C) 6.05
D) 4.76
In this case, you're mixing a weak acid with it's weak conjugate base.
This will create a buffer solution, for which we will use the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation to solve for pH.
First we need the moles of the acid and the base.
[HC2H3O2] = 6 mL x (1 L/1000 mL) x (0.878 mol/1L) = 0.00527 mol
[NaC2H3O2] = 6 mL x (1 L/1000 mL) x (1.65 mol/1L) = 0.00990 mol*
Ka = 10-14/Kb = 10^-14/(5.71x10-10)
pH = -log(Ka) + log(base/acid) = -log(0.0000175) + log(0.00990/0.00527)
pH = 5.03
*since we were using the same volume, we could have just plugged in molarity (before mixing) as a short-cut, or we could have calculated millimoles by not dividing by 1000, as a different shortcut.

33) You add 700 mL of a 0.180 M KOH solution to 500 mL of a 0.140 M HC2H3O2 solution (Ka for HC2H3O2 is 1.8x10-5).  What will be the pH of that solution?
A) 12.8
B) 1.25
C) 9.16
D) 12.7*
Molarity strong base
700 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.18 mol/L) = 0.126 mol KOH
0.126 mol KOH / 1.20 L = 0.105 M

Molarity weak acid
500 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.14 mol/L) = 0.0700 mol HC2H3O2
0.0700 mol HC2H3O2 / 1.20 L = 0.0583 M

Since there is more strong base than weak acid, the pH will be based on the remaining strong base.
Subtract the molarity of weak acid from the molarity of strong base to get the remaining strong base.
0.105 M HI - 0.0583 M C6H5NH2 = 0.0467 M KOH remaining
pOH = -log(0.0467)
pOH 1.33
pH = 14 - pOH = 14 - 1.33 = 12.7

35) You add 600 mL of a 0.12 M LiOH solution to 800 mL of a 0.198 M HCl solution.  What will be the pH of that solution?
A) 1.29
B) 1.06
C) 0.946
D) 1.21*
Molarity of strong base:
600 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.12 mol/L) = 0.0720 mol
0.0720 mol / 1.40 L = 0.0514 M
Molarity of strong acid:
800 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.198 mol/L) = 0.158 mol
0.158 mol / 1.40 L = 0.113 M

Since there is more strong acid than strong base, subtracting the molarity of the base from the molarity of the acid will tell us what the remaining molarity of strong acid will be.
0.113 M  HCl - 0.0514 M LiOH = 0.0617 M HCl remaining
pH = 1.21

36) You add 500 mL of a 0.120 M HCl solution to 500 mL of a 0.120 M NH3 solution (Kb for NH3 is 1.8x10-5).  What will be the pH of that solution?
A) 5.24*
B) 4.74
C) 2.98
D) 9.26

moles of NH4+ = 500 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.12 mol/L) = 0.0600
[NH4+] = 0.0600 / 1.00 L = 0.0600 M
  NH4+ + H2O ⇄ NH3 H3O+
beg 0.0600       
change - x   + x + x
end 0.0600 -x   x x

Ka = 10-14/ 1.8x10-5 = 5.56x10-10
Ka = 5.56x10-10 = x2/(0.0600 -x)
Since 0.0600 / 5.56x10-10 = 1.08x108, the small x assumption is valid
5.56x10-10 = x2/(0.0600)
(0.0600) x 5.56x10-10 = x2
√(3.33x10-11) = x  
x = 5.77x10-6

39) If you have a 1.0 L buffer solution with 0.10 M acetic acid (Ka 1.75x10-5) and 0.10 M sodium acetate, then what will be the pH after adding 0.0800 moles of solid HCl (assume no change in volume)?
A) 3.80*
B) 4.76
C) 5.71
D) 3.51
Beginning moles:
1L x (0.10 mol acetic acid/L) = 0.10 mol acetic acid
1L x (0.10 mol sodium acetate/L) = 0.10 mol sodium acetate

Moles after adding 0.08 moles of HCl:
0.08 moles of HCl will react with 0.08 moles of acetate to produce 0.08 moles of acetic acid
0.10 mol acetic acid +0.08 mol = 0.180 mol acetic acid
0.10 mol sodium acetate -0.08 mol = 0.0200 mol acetic acid
pH = -log(1.75*10-5) + log(0.0200)/(0.180)
pH = 3.80

45) You add 600 mL of a 0.140 M HBr solution to 800 mL of a 0.170 M CH3NH2 solution (Kb for CH3NH2 is 4.38x10-4).  What will be the pH of that solution?
A) 10.0
B) 10.7
C) 10.4*
D) 3.57
Mol strong acid:
600 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.14 mol/L) = 0.0840 mol HBr

Mol weak acid:
800 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.17 mol/L) = 0.136 mol CH3NH2

Since there are more moles of weak base than strong acid, the strong acid will be consumed and produce an equal number of moles of weak acid.  The remaining moles of weak base is the difference between the starting moles of weak acid and strong base.

Mol weak acid remaining:
0.0520

Mol weak base produced:
0.0840 mol CH3NH3+

Since we wind up with an amount of a weak acid and it's conjugate weak base this is a buffer solution, and the Henderson–Hasselbalch can be used to solve for pH.
Ka = 10-14/(4.38x10-4) = 2.28x10-11
pH = -log(2.28x10-11) + log(0.0840/0.0520)
pH = 10.4
