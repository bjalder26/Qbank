3) How much energy is required to heat 37.5 g H2O from a liquid at 40.2°C to a gas at 130°C?
ΔHvap = 40.7 kJ/mol
Cliquid = 4.184 \( \frac{J}{g°C} \) 
Cgas = 2.01 \( \frac{J}{g°C} \)  
Csolid = 2.09 \( \frac{J}{g°C} \) 
Tmelting = 0°C 
Tboiling = 100°C
A) 101 kJ
B) 96.3 kJ*
C) 1540 kJ
D) 1.17x104 kJ

To calculate the energy required to heat water from a liquid at 40.2°C to a gas at 130°C, we need to consider the following steps:

  1. Heating the liquid water from its initial temperature to its boiling point.
  2. Vaporizing the liquid water at its boiling point to gas.
  3. Heating the water vapor from it's boiling point to 130°C.

Step 1: Heating the liquid water First, we calculate the energy required to heat the liquid water from 40.2°C to its boiling point at 100°C.

q1 = m x Cliquid x ΔT1

where: q1 = energy required (in joules) m = mass of water (in grams)
Cliquid = specific heat capacity of liquid water (in J/g-°C)
ΔT1 = change in temperature (final temperature - initial temperature)
Cgas = specific heat capacity of liquid water (in J/g-°C)

Given: m = 37.5 g
Cliquid = 4.184 J/g-°C
ΔT1 = 100°C - 40.2°C = 59.8°C

q1 = (37.5 g) x (4.184 J/g-°C) x (59.8°C)
q1 ≈ 9380 J x \( \frac{1 kJ}{1000 J} \) = 9.38 kJ

Step 2: Vaporizing the liquid water Next, we calculate the energy required to vaporize the liquid water at its boiling point.

q2 = n x ΔHvap

where: q2 = energy required (in joules)
n = number of moles of water
ΔHvap = enthalpy of vaporization (in J/mol)

To calculate the number of moles of water, we use the molar mass of water (H2O), which is approximately 18.02 g/mol.

mol = 37.5 x \( \frac{1 mol H_2O}{18.02 g H_2O} \) ≈ 2.08 mol

Given: ΔHvap = 40.7 kJ/mol 

q2 = (2.08 mol) * (40.7 kJ/mol) ≈ 84.7 kJ

Step 3: Heating the water vapor, we calculate the energy required to heat the water vapor from 100°C to 130°C.

q3 = m x Cliquid x ΔT2

where: q3 = energy required (in J) m = mass of water (in grams)
Cgas = specific heat capacity of liquid water (in J/g-°C)
ΔT1 = change in temperature (final temperature - initial temperature)

Given: m = 37.5 g
Cliquid = 2.01 J/g-°C
ΔT1 = 130°C - 100°C = 30.0°C

q3 = (37.5 g) x (2.01 J/g-°C) x (30.0°C)
q3 ≈ 2260 J x \( \frac{1 kJ}{1000 J} \) = 2.26 kJ

Total energy required: The total energy required is the sum of q1, q2 and q3.

Total energy = q1 + q2 + q3 ≈ 9.38 kJ + 84.7 kJ + 2.26 kJ

Total energy ≈ 96.3 kJ

11) Determine the normal boiling point (at 760 mm Hg) of a substance whose vapor pressure is 52.5 mm Hg at 50.5°C and has a ΔHvap of 27.1 kJ/mol.
A) 52.7 K
B) 324 K
C) 441 K*
D) 256 K

To determine the normal boiling point of a substance, we need to find the temperature at which its vapor pressure is equal to the atmospheric pressure at 760 mm Hg.

Given:
T1: 50.5°C
T2: ?
P1: 52.5 mm Hg
P2 = 760 mm Hg (normal pressure) 
ΔHvap = 27.1 kJ/mol
R = gas constant (8.3145 J/mol·K)

To calculate the normal boiling point, we can use the Clausius-Clapeyron equation:

ln(P2/P1) = -(ΔHvap/R) * (1/T2 - 1/T1)

Converting the given values to K and J:
T1: 50.5°C + 273.15 = 324 K
ΔHvap = 27.1 kJ/mol = 27.1 × 1000J/1 kJ = 2.71x104 J/mol

ln(P2/P1) = -(ΔHvap/R) * (1/T2 - 1/T1)
ln(760 mm Hg/ 52.5 mm Hg) = -(2.71x104 J/mol / 8.3145 J/mol·K) * (1/T2 - 1/324 K)

Simplifying: ln(14.5) = -3260 K * (1/T2 - 1/324 K)

Now, let's solve for T2:
 -0.00082 K = (1/T2 - 1/324 K)
 -0.00082 1/K = 1/T2 - 1/324 K
 -0.00082 1/K + 1/324 1/K = 1/T2 
0.00227 1/K = 1/T2

Taking the inverse of both sides:
441 K = T2

32) The enthalpy of solution for a compound is -35 kJ/mol, and the enthalpy of hydration for the compound's ion X- is -330 kJ/mol. Calculate the lattice energy (in kJ/mol) of the compound.
A) -295 kJ/mol
B) 295 kJ/mol*
C) -365 kJ/mol
D) 365 kJ/mol

To solve this problem, we can use the following equation:

ΔHsolution = ΔHlattice + ΔHhydration

Where: ΔHsolution = Enthalpy of solution
ΔHlattice = Lattice energy
ΔHhydration = Enthalpy of hydration

We are given: ΔHsolution = -35 kJ/mol
ΔHhydration = -330 kJ/mol

Rearranging the equation, we can solve for ΔHlattice:

ΔHlattice = ΔHsolution - ΔHhydration

Plugging in the given values:

ΔHlattice = -35 kJ/mol - (-330 kJ/mol) = -35 kJ/mol + 35.0 kJ/mol = 295 kJ/mol

36) (SLO 1.6) What is the change in boiling point of a solution of 33.9 g K2SO4 (174.3 g/mol) in 792 g of water? Kb for water is 0.512°C/m.  Assume ideal behavior.
A) 21.9°C
B) 0.377°C*
C) 0.126°C
D) 0.000126°C

To calculate the change in boiling point of a solution, we can use the equation:

ΔTb = i x Kb x m

where: i = van't Hoff factor
ΔTb = change in boiling point
Kb = boiling point elevation constant (0.512 °C/m for water)
m = molality of the solution (moles of solute per kilogram of solvent)

First, we need to calculate the molality of the solution, which is the number of moles of solute per kilogram of solvent.

 33.9 g K2SO4 x (1 mol K2SO4)/(174.3 g K2SO4) = 0.194 mol K2SO4

Step 2: Calculate the molality of the solution: Molality = moles of solute / mass of solvent (in kg) Mass of solvent (water) = 792 g x 1 kg/1000 g = 0.792 kg 

Molality =0.194 mol K2SO4 mol / 0.792 kg ≈ 0.246  M

Step 3: van't Hoff factor
The van't Hoff factor for K2SO4 will be estimated at 3 because  K2SO4 contains 3 ions.

Step 3: Calculate the change in boiling point: ΔTb = i x Kb x m ΔTb = 3 x 0.512 °C/m * 0.246  M ≈ 0.377  M °C

39) (SLO 1.3) The Henry’s law constant for oxygen gas in water at 20ºC is 14 x 10-4 M/atm. When the partial pressure of oxygen above a sample of water is 0.728 atm, the concentration of oxygen in the water is ______ M.

C = k * P

where: C is the concentration of the gas in the liquid (in Molarity, M) k is the Henry's law constant for the specific gas-solvent system (in M/atm) P is the partial pressure of the gas above the liquid (in atm)

Given: Henry's law constant (k) for oxygen gas in water = 14 x 10-4 M/atm
Partial pressure of oxygen (P) = 0.728 atm

C = (14 x 10-4 M/atm) * (0.728 atm) C ≈ 0.00102 M

40) (SLO 1.1) The osmotic pressure of a solution formed by dissolving 39.7 mg of Aspirin (C9H8O4) (180.16 g/mol) in enough water to make 0.300 L of solution at 30°C is _____ atm.
A) 0.0183*
B) 0.00548
C) 0.00181
D) 18.3

Given: Mass of Aspirin = 39.7 mg x 1 g/1000 mg= 0.0397 g
Molar mass of Aspirin (C9H8O4) = 180.16 g/mol
Volume of solution (V) = 0.300 L

First, calculate the number of moles of Aspirin:

moles of Aspirin = 0.0236 g x (1mol Aspirin)/(180.16 g Aspirin) ≈ 0.000220 mol

Next, calculate the molarity (M) of the solution:

Molarity (M) = moles of solute / volume of solution Molarity (M) = 0.0000220 mol / 0.300 L
Molarity (M) ≈  0.000735 M

Now we can calculate the osmotic pressure:

π = MRT

where: M is the molarity of the solution R is the ideal gas constant (0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K)) T is the temperature in Kelvin (K)

Given: Temperature (T) = 30°C +273.15 = 303.15 K

π = (0.000735) * (0.08206 L·atm/(mol·K)) * (303.15 K) π ≈ 0.0183 atm
