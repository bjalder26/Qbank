1) How much energy is required to decompose 654 g of PCl3, according to the reaction below? The molar mass of PCl3 is 137.32 g/mol and may be useful.

4 PCl3(g) → P4(s) + 6 Cl2(g), ΔH°rxn = +1207 kJ

A) 5750 kJ
B) 1.97x105 kJ
C) 4.14x104 kJ
D) 1440 kJ*
The chemical equation tells us +1207 kJ are required to decompose 4 moles of PCl3.  So once we have the moles, we can use that as a conversion factor to calculate the kJ required.
654 g PCl3 x (1 mol PCl3 / 137.32 g PCl3) x (1207 kJ / 4 mol PCl3) = 1440 kJ

2) Which of the following would have the lowest lattice energy?
A) CaO
B) Na2O*
C) BeO
D) MgO
There are two parts that are involved in lattice energy.  One is the charge on the ions (this is the bigger priority), and the other is the distance between the ions.  This is only testing on knowing that the larger the charge on the ions, the larger the lattice energy.  Since Na has the lowest charge, Na2O will have the lowest lattice energy.

3) A 5.32 g sample of aniline (C6H5NH2, molar mass = 93.13 g/mol) was combusted in a coffee cup (constant pressure) calorimeter with a heat capacity of 5.11 kJ/°C. If the temperature rose from 26.2°C to 60.4°C, determine the value of ΔH°combustion for aniline (for this problem the water is considered part of the calorimeter, so consider all the heat produced as being absorbed by the calorimeter).
A) 5400 (3 sf) kJ/mol
B) -3060 kJ/mol*
C) -5400 (3 sf) kJ/mol
D) 3060 kJ/mol
The heat (enthalpy) of combustion is equal to the heat given off per mol.
ΔH°combustion = qrxn/n
The heat given off is equal, but opposite to the heat absorbed by the calorimeter (remember the water is considered part of the calorimeter in this problem).
qrxn = -qcal 
qcal = CΔT

Lets solve for the heat absorbed by the calorimeter.
qcal = CΔT = 5.11 kJ/°C x (60.4°C - 26.2°C) = 175 Kj

Now the heat given off by the reaction.
 qrxn = -qcal = -175 kJ

Next lets solve for the moles of reactant (n).
5.32 g x (1 mol / 93.13 g) = 0.0571 mol 

Finally the heat (enthalpy) of combustion.
ΔH°combustion = qrxn/n =  -175 kJ / 0.0571 mol = -3060 kJ/mol

4) What is the final temperature of a gold sample (mass = 335 g) that has a starting temperature of 390 K but loses 5.99 kJ of heat. The specific heat capacity of gold is 0.129 J/g°C.
A) 404 K
B) 529 K
C) 251 K*
D) 376 K
The specific heat formula is q = mcsΔT, (heat = mass x specific heat capacity x change in temperature), or q = mcs(Tf - Ti).
In this case we're solving for final temperature.  Dividing both sides by mcs gives:
q/(mcs) = Tf - Ti, adding Ti to both sides gives:
Ti + q/(mcs) = Tf
Given:
q = -5.99 Kj, converted to J: -5.99 kJ x (1000 J / 1 kJ ) = -5990 J
mass = 335 g
cs = 0.129 J/g°C
Ti = 390 K
Tf = Ti + q/(mcs) = 390 K + (-5990 J)/(335 x  0.129 J/g°C) = 251 K
