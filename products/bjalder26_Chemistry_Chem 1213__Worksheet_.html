2) What would be the molarity of a solution formed by dissolving 20.2 g of NaCl in enough water to yield 500.0 mL of solution?
A) 1.45x104 M
B) 14.5 M
C) 0.000691 M
D) 0.691 M*

To calculate the molarity of a solution, we need to determine the number of moles of solute (NaCl) and then divide it by the volume of the solution in liters.

First, let's calculate the number of moles of NaCl:
Molar mass of NaCl = 58.44 g/mol
Moles of NaCl = Mass of NaCl/ Molar mass of NaCl= 20.2 g / 58.44  g/mol = 0.346 moles

Next, we convert the volume of the solution from milliliters to liters: Volume of solution = 500.0 mL x (1 L / 1000 mL) = 0.500 L

Finally, we calculate the molarity using the formula: Molarity (M) = Moles of solute / Volume of solution (in liters) Molarity = 0.346 moles / 0.500 L = 0.691 M

3) How many grams of sucrose (C12H22O11, molar mass = 342.30 g/mol) are contained 900 mL of a 0.350 M glucose solution?
A) 0.000920 g
B) 108 g*
C) 1.90x1023 g
D) 1.33 g

To calculate the mass of sucrose in a given solution, we need to know the volume of the solution and the molarity of the glucose solution.

Given:
Volume of solution = 900 mL
Molarity of glucose solution = 0.350 M

First, we need to convert the volume from milliliters to liters: Volume of solution =  900 mL x (1L / 1000 mL) = 0.0350 L

Next, we can calculate the number of moles of glucose using the formula: Moles of glucose = Liters of solution × Molarity

Moles of glucose = 0.0350 L × 0.350 M = 0.315 moles

Now, we can use the molar mass of sucrose (342.30 g/mol) to calculate the mass of sucrose: Mass of sucrose = Moles of glucose × Molar mass of sucrose

Mass of sucrose = 0.315 moles × 342.30 g/mol = 108 grams

4) According to the following reaction, how many grams of KCl solution is required to react exactly with 25.0 mL of 0.452 M Zn(ClO3)2 solution?
2 KCl(aq) + Zn(ClO3)2(aq) → ZnCl2(s) + 2 KClO3(aq)
A) 0.842 g
B) 1.68 g*
C) 0.0226 g
D) 0.303 g

To determine the amount of KCl solution required to react with a given volume of Zn(ClO3)2 solution, we need to use the stoichiometry of the balanced chemical equation.
Given:
Volume of Zn(ClO3)2 solution = 25.0 mL
Molarity of Zn(ClO3)2 solution = 0.452 M
Molar mass of KCl = 74.55 g/mol

From the balanced chemical equation: 2 KCl(aq) + Zn(ClO3)2(aq) → ZnCl2(s) + 2 KClO3(aq)

First, calculate the number of moles of Zn(ClO3)2 in the given solution:
Moles of Zn(ClO3)2 = Volume (in liters) × Molarity
Volume of Zn(ClO3)2 solution = 25.0 mL × (1 L / 1000 mL) = 0.0250 L of Zn(ClO3)2
Moles of Zn(ClO3)2 = 0.0250 L × 0.452 M = 0.0113 moles

Since the stoichiometric ratio is 1:2 between Zn(ClO3)2 and KCl, we need twice the number of moles of KCl to react completely.
Moles of KCl = 0.0113 Moles Zn(ClO3)2 × (2 mol KCl / 1 mol Zn(ClO3)2) = 0.0226 moles

Finally, we can calculate the mass of KCl:
Mass of KCl = Moles of KCl × Molar mass of KCl
Mass of KCl = 0.0226 moles × 74.55 g/mol = 1.68 grams

5) What is the oxidation state of S in SO42-?
A) +4
B) +6*
C) +2
D) +8

To determine the oxidation state of sulfur in SO42-, we consider the overall charge of the ion and the known oxidation states of other elements involved.

In the sulfate ion (SO42-), the oxygen atoms have a known oxidation state of -2. The overall charge of the ion is -2 as well. Since the sum of the oxidation states in an ion must equal the overall charge, we can calculate the oxidation state of sulfur.

Let's assume the oxidation state of sulfur as x. We have four oxygen atoms, each with an oxidation state of -2. Therefore:

(x) + 4(-2) = -2

Simplifying the equation:

x - 8 = -2

x = +6

Cd(s) + 2 AgNO3(aq) → Cd(NO3)2(aq) + 2 Ag(s)

The element undergoing reduction is silver (Ag). Reduction is defined as the gain of electrons, and in this reaction, silver ions (Ag+) from silver nitrate (AgNO3) are being reduced to elemental silver (Ag), which is being formed as a solid.
