1) Calculate the concentration of H3O+ (H+) in a solution that has a concentration of OH- of 3.39x10-4 M at 25°C.
A) 3.54x10-11 M
B) 2.95x10-11 M*
C) 3.01x10-19 M
D) 2.65x10-11 M
[H+][OH-] = 10-14 at 25°C. 
[H+] = 10-14/[OH-]
10-14/3.39x10-4 = 2.95x10-11 M

2) If a solution has a pOH of 10.3, then what is the hydronium (hydrogen) ion concentration at 25°C?
A) 5.01x10-11 M
B) 3.70 M
C) 2.00x1010 M
D) 0.000200 M*
pH = 14 - pOH
pH = 3.70
[OH-,H+] = 10-pH
[OH-,H+] = 10-3.70
[OH-,H+] = 0.000200 M

3) Which of the following is the strongest Lewis acid (best at giving up H+ or binding OH-)?
A) K+
B) Li+
C) HF*
D) Na+
The strongest acid, will have the weakest conjugate base.  The conjugate bases of Na+, Li+, and K+ are all strong bases (NaOH, LiOH, and KOH).  Thus the answer is HF.

4) Which of the following chemicals are amphoteric?
A) NaOH
B) H3O+
C) H2O*
D) SO2-
Amphoteric (amph- like amphibian, means both) means that it can act as either an acid or a base.  What you should be looking for is a chemical with an acidic hydrogen (typically it will have the hydrogen listed first or a carboxylic acid group -COOH), that can also accept an additional hydrogen (e.g. water or negatively charged).  Thus H2O can be classified as amphoteric.

5) Determine the pOH of a 0.000804 M NaOH solution.
A) 3.40
B) 1.24x10-11
C) 10.9
D) 3.09*
Since NaOH is a strong base, assume the concentration of NaOH is the concentration of OH-.
pOH = -log[OH-]
pOH = -log(0.000804)
pOH = 3.09

12) You add 400 mL of a 0.140 M LiOH solution to 400 mL of a 0.140 M HC2H3O2 solution (Ka for HC2H3O2 is 1.75x10-5).  What will be the pH of that solution?
A) 8.80*
B) 9.15
C) 5.09
D) 3.60
400 x (1L/1000mL) x (0.14 mol/L) = 0.0560 mol LiOH
400 x (1L/1000mL) x (0.14 mol/L) = 0.0560 mol  HC2H3O2

0.0560 mol of LiOH will react with 0.0560 mol of HC2H3O2 to produce 0.0560 mol of C2H3O2-.

0.0560 mol / 0.800 L = 0.0700 M
R  C2H3O2- + H2O ⇄ HC2H3O2 +  OH-
I 0.0700   0 0
C - x   + x + x
E 0.0700 - x   x x

Kb = 10-14/1.75x10-5 = 5.71x10-10
Kb = 5.71x10-10 = x2/(0.0700 - x), the small x assumption is valid because 0.0700/5.71x10-10 = 1.22x108
5.71x10-10 = x2/(0.0700)
(0.0700) x 5.71x10-10 = x2
(0.0700) x 5.71x10-10 = x2
4.00x10-11 = x2
√(4.00x10-11) = x
6.32x10-6 = x
pOH = -log(6.32x10-6) = 5.20
pH = 14 - 5.20 = 8.80

14) If you are using an acid and it's conjugate base to prepare a buffer that will be effective at a pH of 8.00 at 25°C, which of the following Ka values would be best for your acid?
A) 9.79x10-10
B) 1.02x10-10
C) 1.09x10-8*
D) 9.19x10-8
You want -log(Ka) to be as close to your desired pH as possible, because then the buffer solution will have an equal amount of acid and conjugate base (the 1/2 equivalence point).
- log(1.09x10-8) = 7.96, which is the closest to the pH of 8.

15) You add 500 mL of a 0.110 M H2SO4 solution to 700 mL of a 0.160 M KOH solution.  What will be the pH of that solution?
A) 12.5
B) 1.32
C) 13.1
D) 12.7*
Molarity of strong acid:
500 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.11 mol/L) = 0.0550 mol
0.0550 mol / 1.20 L = 0.0458 M
Molarity of strong base:
700 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.16 mol/L) = 0.112 mol
0.112 mol / 1.20 L = 0.0933 M

Since there is more strong base than strong acid, subtracting the molarity of the acid from the molarity of the base will tell us what the remaining molarity of strong base will be.
0.0933 M  KOH - 0.0458 M H2SO4 = 0.0475 M KOH remaining
pOH = 1.32
pH = 14 - 1.32 = 12.7

16) What would be the pH of a solution formed by mixing 500 mL of 0.159 M H2CO3 with 500 mL of 1.25 M NaHCO3?  The Kb for HCO3- is 2.2x10-8.
A) 6.35
B) 7.24*
C) 8.29
D) 5.45
In this case, you're mixing a weak acid with it's weak conjugate base.
This will create a buffer solution, for which we will use the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation to solve for pH.
First we need the moles of the acid and the base.
[H2CO3] = 500 mL x (1 L/1000 mL) x (0.159 mol/1L) = 0.0795 mol
[NaHCO3] = 500 mL x (1 L/1000 mL) x (1.25 mol/1L) = 0.625 mol*
Ka = 10-14/Kb = 10^-14/(2.2x10-8)
pH = -log(Ka) + log(base/acid) = -log(4.55x10-7) + log(0.625/0.0795)
pH = 7.24
*since we were using the same volume, we could have just plugged in molarity (before mixing) as a short-cut, or we could have calculated millimoles by not dividing by 1000, as a different shortcut.

17) You add 500 mL of a 0.150 M HI solution to 800 mL of a 0.200 M C6H5NH2 solution (Kb for C6H5NH2 is 3.8x10-10).  What will be the pH of that solution?
A) 4.63*
B) 9.37
C) 5.03
D) 4.43
Mol strong acid:
500 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.15 mol/L) = 0.0750 mol HI

Mol weak acid:
800 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.20 mol/L) = 0.160 mol C6H5NH2

Since there are more moles of weak base than strong acid, the strong acid will be consumed and produce an equal number of moles of weak acid.  The remaining moles of weak base is the difference between the starting moles of weak acid and strong base.

Mol weak acid remaining:
0.0850

Mol weak base produced:
0.0750 mol C6H5NH3+

Since we wind up with an amount of a weak acid and it's conjugate weak base this is a buffer solution, and the Henderson–Hasselbalch can be used to solve for pH.
Ka = 10-14/(3.8x10-10) = 0.0000263
pH = -log(0.0000263) + log(0.0750/0.0850)
pH = 4.63

18) How many moles of  Ba3(PO4)2 can be dissolved in 3.00 L of solution. Ksp ( Ba3(PO4)2) = 6.00x10-39.
A) 5.86x10-7 moles
B) 2.67x10-8 moles*
C) 2.24x10-20 moles
D) 3.32x10-8 moles
Ksp ( Ba3(PO4)2) = 6.00x10-39 = (3x)3(2x)2
6.00x10-39 = 108x5
x = 5√(6.00*10^-39/108) = 8.89x10-9 M
8.89x10-9 M x 3.00 L = 2.67x10-8 moles

19) You add 700 mL of a 0.180 M HBr solution to 600 mL of a 0.150 M CH3NH2 solution (Kb for CH3NH2 is 4.38x10-4).  What will be the pH of that solution?
A) 3.21
B) 3.36
C) 1.44
D) 1.56*
Molarity strong acid
700 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.18 mol/L) = 0.126 mol HBr
0.126 mol HBr / 1.30 L = 0.0969 M

Molarity weak base
600 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.15 mol/L) = 0.0900 mol CH3NH2
0.0900 mol CH3NH2 / 1.30 L = 0.0692 M

Since there is more strong acid than weak base, the pH will be based on the remaining strong acid.
Subtract the molarity of weak base from the molarity of strong acid to get the remaining strong acid.
0.0969 M HBr - 0.0692 M CH3NH2 = 0.0277 M HBr remaining
pH = -log(0.0277)
pH 1.56

21) (SLO 4.6) What is the [CH3CO2-]/[CH3CO2H] ratio necessary to make a buffer solution with a pH of 5.87? 
Ka = 1.8x10-5 for CH3CO2H.
A) 0.075 : 1
B) 11 : 1
C) 7.4 : 1
D) 13 : 1*
pH = -log(Ka) + log(base/acid)
5.87 = -log(1.8x10-5) + log([CH3CO2-]/[CH3CO2H])
5.87 = 4.74 + log([CH3CO2-]/[CH3CO2H])
5.87 - 4.74 = log([CH3CO2-]/[CH3CO2H])
1.13 = log([CH3CO2-]/[CH3CO2H])
101.13  = [CH3CO2-]/[CH3CO2H]
[CH3CO2-]/[CH3CO2H] = 13 : 1
