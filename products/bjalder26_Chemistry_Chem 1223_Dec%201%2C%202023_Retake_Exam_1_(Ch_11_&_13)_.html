3) Determine the normal boiling point (at 760 mm Hg) of a substance whose vapor pressure is 47.5 mm Hg at 45.3°C and has a ΔHvap of 26.2 kJ/mol.
A) 319 K
B) 442 K*
C) 47.2 K
D) 249 K

To determine the normal boiling point of a substance, we need to find the temperature at which its vapor pressure is equal to the atmospheric pressure at 760 mm Hg.

Given:
T1: 45.3°C
T2: ?
P1: 47.5 mm Hg
P2 = 760 mm Hg (normal pressure) 
ΔHvap = 26.2 kJ/mol
R = gas constant (8.3145 J/mol·K)

To calculate the normal boiling point, we can use the Clausius-Clapeyron equation:

ln(P2/P1) = -(ΔHvap/R) * (1/T2 - 1/T1)

Converting the given values to K and J:
T1: 45.3°C + 273.15 = 318 K
ΔHvap = 26.2 kJ/mol = 26.2 × 1000J/1 kJ = 2.62x104 J/mol

ln(P2/P1) = -(ΔHvap/R) * (1/T2 - 1/T1)
ln(760 mm Hg/ 47.5 mm Hg) = -(2.62x104 J/mol / 8.3145 J/mol·K) * (1/T2 - 1/318 K)

Simplifying: ln(16.0) = -3150 K * (1/T2 - 1/318 K)

Now, let's solve for T2:
 -0.00088 K = (1/T2 - 1/318 K)
 -0.00088 1/K = 1/T2 - 1/318 K
 -0.00088 1/K + 1/318 1/K = 1/T2 
0.00226 1/K = 1/T2

Taking the inverse of both sides:
442 K = T2

9) How much energy is required to heat 36.7 g H2O from a liquid at 54.3°C to a gas at 130°C?
ΔHvap = 40.7 kJ/mol
Cliquid = 4.184 \( \frac{J}{g°C} \) 
Cgas = 2.01 \( \frac{J}{g°C} \)  
Csolid = 2.09 \( \frac{J}{g°C} \) 
Tmelting = 0°C 
Tboiling = 100°C
A) 9310 kJ
B) 1500 (3 sf) kJ
C) 92.1 kJ*
D) 101 kJ

To calculate the energy required to heat water from a liquid at 54.3°C to a gas at 130°C, we need to consider the following steps:

  1. Heating the liquid water from its initial temperature to its boiling point.
  2. Vaporizing the liquid water at its boiling point to gas.
  3. Heating the water vapor from it's boiling point to 130°C.

Step 1: Heating the liquid water First, we calculate the energy required to heat the liquid water from 54.3°C to its boiling point at 100°C.

q1 = m x Cliquid x ΔT1

where: q1 = energy required (in joules) m = mass of water (in grams)
Cliquid = specific heat capacity of liquid water (in J/g-°C)
ΔT1 = change in temperature (final temperature - initial temperature)
Cgas = specific heat capacity of liquid water (in J/g-°C)

Given: m = 36.7 g
Cliquid = 4.184 J/g-°C
ΔT1 = 100°C - 54.3°C = 45.7°C

q1 = (36.7 g) x (4.184 J/g-°C) x (45.7°C)
q1 ≈ 7020 J x \( \frac{1 kJ}{1000 J} \) = 7.02 kJ

Step 2: Vaporizing the liquid water Next, we calculate the energy required to vaporize the liquid water at its boiling point.

q2 = n x ΔHvap

where: q2 = energy required (in joules)
n = number of moles of water
ΔHvap = enthalpy of vaporization (in J/mol)

To calculate the number of moles of water, we use the molar mass of water (H2O), which is approximately 18.02 g/mol.

mol = 36.7 x \( \frac{1 mol H_2O}{18.02 g H_2O} \) ≈ 2.04 mol

Given: ΔHvap = 40.7 kJ/mol 

q2 = (2.04 mol) * (40.7 kJ/mol) ≈ 82.9 kJ

Step 3: Heating the water vapor, we calculate the energy required to heat the water vapor from 100°C to 130°C.

q3 = m x Cliquid x ΔT2

where: q3 = energy required (in J) m = mass of water (in grams)
Cgas = specific heat capacity of liquid water (in J/g-°C)
ΔT1 = change in temperature (final temperature - initial temperature)

Given: m = 36.7 g
Cliquid = 2.01 J/g-°C
ΔT1 = 130°C - 100°C = 30.0°C

q3 = (36.7 g) x (2.01 J/g-°C) x (30.0°C)
q3 ≈ 2210 J x \( \frac{1 kJ}{1000 J} \) = 2.21 kJ

Total energy required: The total energy required is the sum of q1, q2 and q3.

Total energy = q1 + q2 + q3 ≈ 7.02 kJ + 82.9 kJ + 2.21 kJ

Total energy ≈ 92.1 kJ

21) The enthalpy of solution for a compound is -95 kJ/mol, and the enthalpy of hydration for the compound's ion X- is -430 kJ/mol. Calculate the lattice energy (in kJ/mol) of the compound.
A) -335 kJ/mol
B) -525 kJ/mol
C) 335 kJ/mol*
D) 525 kJ/mol

To solve this problem, we can use the following equation:

ΔHsolution = ΔHlattice + ΔHhydration

Where: ΔHsolution = Enthalpy of solution
ΔHlattice = Lattice energy
ΔHhydration = Enthalpy of hydration

We are given: ΔHsolution = -95 kJ/mol
ΔHhydration = -430 kJ/mol

Rearranging the equation, we can solve for ΔHlattice:

ΔHlattice = ΔHsolution - ΔHhydration

Plugging in the given values:

ΔHlattice = -95 kJ/mol - (-430 kJ/mol) = -95 kJ/mol + 95.0 kJ/mol = 335 kJ/mol

36) (SLO 1.3) The Henry’s law constant for carbon dioxide gas in water at 20ºC is 392 x 10-4 M/atm. When the partial pressure of carbon dioxide above a sample of water is 0.505 atm, the concentration of carbon dioxide in the water is ______ M.
A) 198
B) 0.0198*
C) 0.0776
D) 12.9

Henry's law relates the concentration of a gas in a liquid to its partial pressure above the liquid. The equation for Henry's law is:

C = k * P

where: C is the concentration of the gas in the liquid (in Molarity, M) k is the Henry's law constant for the specific gas-solvent system (in M/atm) P is the partial pressure of the gas above the liquid (in atm)

Given: Henry's law constant (k) for carbon dioxide gas in water = 392 x 10-4 M/atm
Partial pressure of carbon dioxide (P) = 0.505 atm

Using Henry's law, we can calculate the concentration of nitrogen gas in the water (C):

C = (392 x 10-4 M/atm) * (0.505 atm) C ≈ 0.0198 M

37) (SLO 1.1) The osmotic pressure of a solution formed by dissolving 27.4 mg of Aspirin (C9H8O4) (180.16 g/mol) in enough water to make 0.215 L of solution at 30°C is _____ atm.
A) 0.00378
B) 0.00174
C) 0.0176*
D) 17.6

Molar mass of Aspirin (C9H8O4) = 180.16 g/mol
Volume of solution (V) = 0.215 L

First, calculate the number of moles of Aspirin:

moles of Aspirin = 0.0236 g x (1mol Aspirin)/(180.16 g Aspirin) ≈ 0.000152 mol

Next, calculate the molarity (M) of the solution:

Molarity (M) = moles of solute / volume of solution Molarity (M) = 0.0000152 mol / 0.215 L

Now we can calculate the osmotic pressure:

π = MRT

where: M is the molarity of the solution R is the ideal gas constant (0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K)) T is the temperature in Kelvin (K)

Given: Temperature (T) = 30°C +273.15 = 303.15 K

π = (0.000707) * (0.08206 L·atm/(mol·K)) * (303.15 K) π ≈ 0.0176 atm

38) (SLO 1.6) What is the change in boiling point of a solution of 32.2 g Na3PO4 (163.9 g/mol) in 764 g of water? Kb for water is 0.512°C/m.  Assume ideal behavior.
A) 0.527°C*
B) 21.6°C
C) 0.000132°C
D) 0.132°C

To calculate the change in boiling point of a solution, we can use the equation:

ΔTb = i x Kb x m

where: i = van't Hoff factor
ΔTb = change in boiling point
Kb = boiling point elevation constant (0.512 °C/m for water)
m = molality of the solution (moles of solute per kilogram of solvent)

First, we need to calculate the molality of the solution, which is the number of moles of solute per kilogram of solvent.

 32.2 g Na3PO4 x (1 mol Na3PO4)/(163.9 g Na3PO4) = 0.196 mol Na3PO4

Step 2: Calculate the molality of the solution: Molality = moles of solute / mass of solvent (in kg) Mass of solvent (water) = 764 g x 1 kg/1000 g = 0.764 kg 

Molality =0.196 mol Na3PO4 mol / 0.764 kg ≈ 0.257  M

Step 3: van't Hoff factor
The van't Hoff factor for Na3PO4 will be estimated at 4 because  Na3PO4 contains 4 ions.

Step 3: Calculate the change in boiling point: ΔTb = i x Kb x m ΔTb = 4 x 0.512 °C/m * 0.257  M ≈ 0.527  M °C
