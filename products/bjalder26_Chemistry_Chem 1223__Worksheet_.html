1) If you have a 1.0 L buffer solution with 0.10 M acetic acid (Ka 1.75x10-5) and 0.10 M sodium acetate, then what will be the pH after adding 0.0400 moles of solid HCl (assume no change in volume)?
A) 4.26
B) 4.76
C) 4.39*
D) 5.12
Beginning moles:
1L x (0.10 mol acetic acid/L) = 0.10 mol acetic acid
1L x (0.10 mol sodium acetate/L) = 0.10 mol sodium acetate

Moles after adding 0.04 moles of HCl:
0.04 moles of HCl will react with 0.04 moles of acetate to produce 0.04 moles of acetic acid
0.10 mol acetic acid +0.04 mol = 0.140 mol acetic acid
0.10 mol sodium acetate -0.04 mol = 0.0600 mol acetic acid
pH = -log(1.75*10-5) + log(0.0600)/(0.140)
pH = 4.39

2) You add 800 mL of a 0.170 M HBr solution to 500 mL of a 0.130 M CH3NH2 solution (Kb for CH3NH2 is 4.38x10-4).  What will be the pH of that solution?
A) 1.15
B) 3.36
C) 3.04
D) 1.26*
Molarity strong acid
800 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.17 mol/L) = 0.136 mol HBr
0.136 mol HBr / 1.30 L = 0.105 M

Molarity weak base
500 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.13 mol/L) = 0.0650 mol CH3NH2
0.0650 mol CH3NH2 / 1.30 L = 0.0500 M

Since there is more strong acid than weak base, the pH will be based on the remaining strong acid.
Subtract the molarity of weak base from the molarity of strong acid to get the remaining strong acid.
0.105 M HBr - 0.0500 M CH3NH2 = 0.0546 M HBr remaining
pH = -log(0.0546)
pH 1.26

4) What would be the pH of a solution formed by mixing 700 mL of 0.302 M NH4Cl with 700 mL of 1.77 M NH3?  The Kb for NH3 is 1.59x10-5.
A) 9.20
B) 8.95
C) 8.43
D) 9.97*
In this case, you're mixing a weak acid with it's weak conjugate base.
This will create a buffer solution, for which we will use the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation to solve for pH.
First we need the moles of the acid and the base.
[NH4Cl] = 700 mL x (1 L/1000 mL) x (0.302 mol/1L) = 0.211 mol
[NH3] = 700 mL x (1 L/1000 mL) x (1.77 mol/1L) = 1.24 mol*
Ka = 10-14/Kb = 10^-14/(1.59x10-5)
pH = -log(Ka) + log(base/acid) = -log(6.29x10-10) + log(1.24/0.211)
pH = 9.97
*since we were using the same volume, we could have just plugged in molarity (before mixing) as a short-cut, or we could have calculated millimoles by not dividing by 1000, as a different shortcut.

5) You add 700 mL of a 0.190 M KOH solution to 600 mL of a 0.140 M HC2H3O2 solution (Ka for HC2H3O2 is 1.8x10-5).  What will be the pH of that solution?

D) 13.0

Molarity weak acid
600 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.14 mol/L) = 0.0840 mol HC2H3O2
0.0840 mol HC2H3O2 / 1.30 L = 0.0646 M

Since there is more strong base than weak acid, the pH will be based on the remaining strong base.
Subtract the molarity of weak acid from the molarity of strong base to get the remaining strong base.
0.102 M HI - 0.0646 M C6H5NH2 = 0.0377 M KOH remaining
pOH = -log(0.0377)
pOH 1.42
pH = 14 - pOH = 14 - 1.42 = 12.6

8) You add 500 mL of a 0.150 M HI solution to 800 mL of a 0.170 M KOH solution.  What will be the pH of that solution?
A) 1.33
B) 13.1
C) 12.5
D) 12.7*
Molarity of strong acid:
500 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.15 mol/L) = 0.0750 mol
0.0750 mol / 1.30 L = 0.0577 M
Molarity of strong base:
800 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.17 mol/L) = 0.136 mol
0.136 mol / 1.30 L = 0.105 M

Since there is more strong base than strong acid, subtracting the molarity of the acid from the molarity of the base will tell us what the remaining molarity of strong base will be.
0.105 M  KOH - 0.0577 M HI = 0.0469 M KOH remaining
pOH = 1.33
pH = 14 - 1.33 = 12.7

9) If you are using an acid and it's conjugate base to prepare a buffer that will be effective at a pH of 6.00 at 25°C, which of the following Ka values would be best for your acid?
A) 9.83x10-9
B) 9.63x10-6
C) 1.08x10-6*
D) 1.04x10-7
You want -log(Ka) to be as close to your desired pH as possible, because then the buffer solution will have an equal amount of acid and conjugate base (the 1/2 equivalence point).
- log(1.08x10-6) = 5.97, which is the closest to the pH of 6.

10) You add 500 mL of a 0.100 M NaOH solution to 500 mL of a 0.100 M HClO solution (Ka for HClO is 3.5x10-8).  What will be the pH of that solution?
A) 3.84
B) 10.4
C) 6.15
D) 10.1*
500 x (1L/1000mL) x (0.10 mol/L) = 0.0500 mol NaOH
500 x (1L/1000mL) x (0.10 mol/L) = 0.0500 mol  HClO

0.0500 mol of NaOH will react with 0.0500 mol of HClO to produce 0.0500 mol of ClO-.

0.0500 mol / 1.00 L = 0.0500 M
R  ClO- + H2O ⇄ HClO +  OH-
I 0.0500   0 0
C - x   + x + x
E 0.0500 - x   x x

Kb = 10-14/3.5x10-8 = 2.86x10-7
Kb = 2.86x10-7 = x2/(0.0500 - x), the small x assumption is valid because 0.0500/2.86x10-7 = 1.75x105
2.86x10-7 = x2/(0.0500)
(0.0500) x 2.86x10-7 = x2
(0.0500) x 2.86x10-7 = x2
1.43x10-8 = x2
√(1.43x10-8) = x
0.000120 = x
pOH = -log(0.000120) = 3.92
pH = 14 - 3.92 = 10.1

I   0 0.19
C   + x + x
E   x 0.19 + x

16) You add 500 mL of a 0.140 M HI solution to 500 mL of a 0.140 M C6H5NH2 solution (Kb for C6H5NH2 is 3.8x10-10).  What will be the pH of that solution?
A) 5.29
B) 9.42
C) 2.87*
D) 4.58
In this problem, there is an equal amount of strong acid, and weak base.  This means the strong acid and strong base will both be completely consumed.  That will leave the conjugate weak acid.
HI + C6H5NH2 ⇄ C6H5NH3+ + I- 
moles of C6H5NH3+ = 500 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.14 mol/L) = 0.0700
[C6H5NH3+] = 0.0700 / 1.00 L = 0.0700 M
  C6H5NH3+ + H2O ⇄ C6H5NH2 H3O+
beg 0.0700       
change - x   + x + x
end 0.0700 -x   x x

Ka = 10-14/ 3.8x10-10 = 0.0000263
Ka = 0.0000263 = x2/(0.0700 -x)
Since 0.0700 / 0.0000263 = 2660, the small x assumption is valid
0.0000263 = x2/(0.0700)
(0.0700) x 0.0000263 = x2
√(1.84x10-6) = x  
x = 0.00136
pH = 2.87

18) You add 500 mL of a 0.130 M HI solution to 800 mL of a 0.170 M C6H5NH2 solution (Kb for C6H5NH2 is 3.8x10-10).  What will be the pH of that solution?
A) 4.42
B) 5.02
C) 9.38
D) 4.62*
Mol strong acid:
500 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.13 mol/L) = 0.0650 mol HI

Mol weak acid:
800 mL x (1L/1000mL) x (0.17 mol/L) = 0.136 mol C6H5NH2

Since there are more moles of weak base than strong acid, the strong acid will be consumed and produce an equal number of moles of weak acid.  The remaining moles of weak base is the difference between the starting moles of weak acid and strong base.

Mol weak acid remaining:
0.0710

Mol weak base produced:
0.0650 mol C6H5NH3+

Since we wind up with an amount of a weak acid and it's conjugate weak base this is a buffer solution, and the Henderson–Hasselbalch can be used to solve for pH.
Ka = 10-14/(3.8x10-10) = 0.0000263
pH = -log(0.0000263) + log(0.0650/0.0710)
pH = 4.62
