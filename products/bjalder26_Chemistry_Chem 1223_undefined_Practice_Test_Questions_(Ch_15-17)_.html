1) The reaction below has a Kp value of 2.48x10-3. What is the value of Kc for this reaction at 660 K?

A) 2.88x10-10
B) 2.13x104*
C) 1.12x10-7
D) 2.88x10-10
Kp = Kc(RT)Δn, solving for Kc:
 Kc = Kp/(RT)Δn =  2.48x10-3/(0.08206 L-atm/mol-K x 660 K)-4
Kc = [2.48*10^-3/((0.08206*660)^(1-2-3))]

2) What is Δn for the following equation in relating Kc to Kp?
H2 (g) + Br2 (g) ⇌ 2 HBr (g)
A) 3
B) -2
C) 1
D) 0*
A change is always final minus initial, so take the moles of gas in the product 2 HBr (g), and subtract the moles of gas on the reactant side H2 (g) + Br2 (g).
2 - 2 = 0

3) Is the following reaction reactant or product favored?
AgCl (s) ⇌ Ag+ (aq) + Cl- (aq)      Kc = 1.8x10-10
A) Reactant favored*
B) Product favored
C) All of the above.
D) None of the above.
When Kc is greater than 1, it is product favored, when Kc is less than 1 it is reactant favored.  This is because Kc is essentially products over reactants.  So a number greater than 1 indicates more products than reactants, whereas a number lower than 1 indicates more reactants than products.

A) A decrease in pressure
B) Adding SO3
C) Removing SO3*
D) An increase in temperature
Adding reactants or removing products will shift a reaction to the right.

5) If Q (the reactant quotient) is greater than the equilibrium constant, then which way will the reaction proceed more quickly?
A) In the forward direction
B) In the reverse direction*
C) All of the above.
D) None of the above.
If Q (the reactant quotient) is greater than the equilibrium constant, then it means we have too much product (numerator) and too little reactant (denominator) to be at equilibrium.  Since reversible reactions will always proceed to equilibrium, the reaction will have to proceed in the reverse direction to produce more reactant, and use up some of the product.

6) At equilibrium, which of the following are true?
A) The reaction stops
B) The concentration of the reactants and products are equal
C) The rate constant of the forward reaction is equal to the equilibrium constant
D) The rate of the forward reaction is equal to the rate of the reverse reaction*
The rate of the forward reaction is equal to the rate of the reverse reaction, which means that the concentration of the reactants and products will remain stable.

11) (SLO 3.5) Identify the change that will always shift the equilibrium to the right.
A) Removing reactant
B) Increasing pressure
C) Decreasing volume
D) Removing product*
Adding reactant will always shift the equilibrium to the right
Removing reactant will always shift the equilibrium to the left
Adding product will always shift the equilibrium to the left
Removing product will always shift the equilibrium to the right
Increasing pressure or decreasing volume will always shift the equilibrium to the side with lower moles of gas
Decreasing pressure ore increasing volume will always shift the equilibrium to the side with higher moles of gas
Increasing temperature will shift the equilibrium to the right in an endothermic reaction, but to the left in an exothermic reaction
Decreasing temperature will shift the equilibrium to the left in an endothermic reaction, but to the right in an endothermic reaction

12) (SLO 3.4) The following reaction is exothermic.  Which change will shift the equilibrium to the left?

B) Lowering the temperature
C) Raising the temperature*
D) All of the above.

In an exothermic reaction, you can treat heat as if it is a product, thus raising the temperature will shift the equilibrium to the left.
Adding a solid does not affect equilibrium.

18) Which of the following is the strongest Lewis acid (best at giving up H+ or binding OH-)?
A) Li+
B) Na+
C) K+
D) NH4+*
The strongest acid, will have the weakest conjugate base.  The conjugate bases of Na+, Li+, and K+ are all strong bases (NaOH, LiOH, and KOH).  Thus the answer is NH4+.

20) Determine the [H3O+] in a 0.506 M HF solution. The Ka of HF is 3.5 × 10-4.
A) 0.000177 M
B) 0.0160 M
C) 0.0133 M*
D) 130. M
Since HF is a weak acid, we need a RICE table, and acid dissociation constant equation.
R HF ⇄ H+ +  F-
I 0.506 0 0
C - x + x + x
E 0.506 - x x x

Ka = 3.5 × 10-4 = [H+][F-]/[HF]
3.5 × 10-4 = x2/(0.506 - x), small x assumption is valid because 0.506/(3.5 × 10-4) = 1450

x = √(0.506 x 3.5 × 10-4)

34) Which of the following chemicals are amphoteric?

B) NH4+
C) Ca(OH)2
D) HPO42-*
Amphoteric (amph- like amphibian, means both) means that it can act as either an acid or a base.  What you should be looking for is a chemical with an acidic hydrogen (typically it will have the hydrogen listed first or a carboxylic acid group -COOH), that can also accept an additional hydrogen (e.g. water or negatively charged).  Thus HPO42- can be classified as amphoteric.

48) If you are using an acid and it's conjugate base to prepare a buffer that will be effective at a pH of 5.00 at 25°C, which of the following Ka values would be best for your acid?

49) What would be the pH of a solution formed by mixing 300 mL of 0.858 M HCN with 300 mL of 1.48 M NaCN?  The Ka for HCN is 6.2x10-10.
A) 8.39
B) 8.97
C) 9.21
D) 9.44*
In this case, you're mixing a weak acid with it's weak conjugate base.
This will create a buffer solution, for which we will use the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation to solve for pH.
First we need the moles of the acid and the base.
[HCN] = 3 mL x (1 L/1000 mL) x (0.858 mol/1L) = 0.00257 mol
[NaCN] = 3 mL x (1 L/1000 mL) x (1.48 mol/1L) = 0.00444 mol*
pH = -log(Ka) + log(base/acid) = -log(6.2x10-10) + log(0.00444/0.00257)
pH = 9.44
*since we were using the same volume, we could have just plugged in molarity (before mixing) as a short-cut, or we could have calculated millimoles by not dividing by 1000, as a different shortcut.
