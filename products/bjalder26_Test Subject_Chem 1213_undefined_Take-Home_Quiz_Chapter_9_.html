1) A 3.56 g sample of aniline (C6H5NH2, molar mass = 93.13 g/mol) was combusted in a coffee cup (constant pressure) calorimeter with a heat capacity of 4.14 kJ/°C. If the temperature rose from 23.1°C to 62.6°C, determine the value of ΔH°combustion for aniline (for this problem the water is considered part of the calorimeter, so consider all the heat produced as being absorbed by the calorimeter).
A) -6780 kJ/mol
B) 6780 kJ/mol
C) 4280 kJ/mol
D) -4280 kJ/mol*
The heat (enthalpy) of combustion is equal to the heat given off per mol.
ΔH°combustion = qrxn/n
The heat given off is equal, but opposite to the heat absorbed by the calorimeter (remember the water is considered part of the calorimeter in this problem).
qrxn = -qcal 
qcal = CΔT

Lets solve for the heat absorbed by the calorimeter.
qcal = CΔT = 4.14 kJ/°C x (62.6°C - 23.1°C) = 164 Kj

Now the heat given off by the reaction.
 qrxn = -qcal = -164 kJ

Next lets solve for the moles of reactant (n).
3.56 g x (1 mol / 93.13 g) = 0.0382 mol 

Finally the heat (enthalpy) of combustion.
ΔH°combustion = qrxn/n =  -164 kJ / 0.0382 mol = -4280 kJ/mol

2) Which of the following would have the highest lattice energy?
A) MgO*
B) Na2O
C) Li2O
D) Li2O
There are two parts that are involved in lattice energy.  One is the charge on the ions (this is the bigger priority), and the other is the distance between the ions.  This is only testing on knowing that the larger the charge on the ions, the larger the lattice energy.  Since Mg has the highest charge, MgO will have the highest lattice energy.

3) What is the final temperature of a aluminum sample (mass = 349 g) that has a starting temperature of 420 K but loses 4.51 kJ of heat. The specific heat capacity of aluminum is 0.900 J/g°C.
A) 406 K*
B) 434 K
C) 419 K
D) 421 K
The specific heat formula is q = mcsΔT, (heat = mass x specific heat capacity x change in temperature), or q = mcs(Tf - Ti).
In this case we're solving for final temperature.  Dividing both sides by mcs gives:
q/(mcs) = Tf - Ti, adding Ti to both sides gives:
Ti + q/(mcs) = Tf
Given:
q = -4.51 Kj, converted to J: -4.51 kJ x (1000 J / 1 kJ ) = -4510 J
mass = 349 g
cs = 0.900 J/g°C
Ti = 420 K
Tf = Ti + q/(mcs) = 420 K + (-4510 J)/(349 x  0.900 J/g°C) = 406 K

5) How much energy is required to decompose 668 g of PCl3, according to the reaction below? The molar mass of PCl3 is 137.32 g/mol and may be useful.

4 PCl3(g) → P4(s) + 6 Cl2(g), ΔH°rxn = +1207 kJ

A) 2.02x105 kJ
B) 5870 kJ
C) 1470 kJ*
D) 4.14x104 kJ
The chemical equation tells us +1207 kJ are required to decompose 4 moles of PCl3.  So once we have the moles, we can use that as a conversion factor to calculate the kJ required.
668 g PCl3 x (1 mol PCl3 / 137.32 g PCl3) x (1207 kJ / 4 mol PCl3) = 1470 kJ
