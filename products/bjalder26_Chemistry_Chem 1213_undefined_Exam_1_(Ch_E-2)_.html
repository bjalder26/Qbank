1) How many significant figures are in the measurement, 4600. m?
A) 3
B) 4*
C) 2
D) 5
E) None of the above.

The measurement 4600. g has four significant figures. Significant figures are the digits in a number that contribute to its precision. When determining the number of significant figures, we consider the following:

  1. Non-zero digits (4 and 6) are always significant.
  2. Leading zeros are never significant.  There are no leading zeros in this problem.
  3. Captured zeros, which are zeros between non-zero digits (1 and 0), are also significant.  There are no captured zeros in this case.
  4. Trailing zeros (the final zeros) are significant only if they are after a decimal point. In this case, since there is a decimal point, the two trailing zeros are considered significant.

To summarize, captured zeros and non-zero digits are significant, leading zeros (zeros before the non-zero digits) are not significant, and trailing zeros (zeros after the non-zero digits) are significant if there is a decimal.  Therefore, the measurement 4600. g  has four significant figures.

10) Which of the following describe a compound?
A) A substance composed of two or more elements in a fixed*
B) where the composition is variable throughout
C) definite proportion
D) where the composition is constant throughout

An element: A substance that can't be chemically broken down into simpler substances. Elements are made up of only one type of atom.

A compound: A substance composed of two or more elements in a fixed, definite proportion. Compounds are chemically bonded together and have a specific chemical formula.

A homogeneous mixture: Two or more substances in variable proportions, where the composition is constant throughout. In a homogeneous mixture, the components are evenly distributed, and the mixture appears uniform at the macroscopic level.

A heterogeneous mixture: Two or more substances in variable proportions, where the composition is variable throughout. In a heterogeneous mixture, the components are not evenly distributed, and the mixture may have visible variations or phases.

To summarize:

  • Element: Can't be chemically broken down into simpler substances.
  • Compound: Composed of two or more elements in a fixed, definite proportion.
  • Homogeneous mixture: Components are in variable proportions, but the composition is constant throughout. Appears uniform.
  • Heterogeneous mixture: Components are in variable proportions, and the composition may vary throughout. Exhibits visible variations.

14) What is the name of the Ti isotope that has 26 neutrons?
A) Titanium-47
B) Titanium-26
C) Titanium-48*
D) Titanium-22

To determine an isotope given its chemical symbol and the number of neutrons, follow these steps:

  1. Identify the chemical symbol: The chemical symbol represents the element. It is typically a one- or two-letter abbreviation, such as H for hydrogen, C for carbon, or Au for gold.

  2. Determine the atomic number: The atomic number of an element corresponds to the number of protons in the nucleus of its atoms. It is usually listed as a subscript to the left of the chemical symbol. You can find the atomic number on the periodic table.

  3. Calculate the mass number: The number of neutrons is given. Add this number to the atomic number to determine the mass number. The mass number represents the total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus.

  4. Write the isotope: Once you know the atomic number and the number of neutrons, you can write the isotope by combining the chemical symbol, atomic number, and mass number. The atomic number is written as a subscript, and the mass number is written as a superscript.

In this problem:

  1. The chemical symbol is Ti.
  2. The atomic number of titanium is 22.
  3. Add the number of neutrons (26 neutrons) to the atomic number (22) to determine the mass number: 26 + 22 = 48.
  4. Write the isotope: Titanium-48

  • Compound: Two or more different elements chemically bonded together.

17) What is the distance between adjacent crests of a wave called?

C) frequency

Frequency: Frequency refers to the number of complete cycles or oscillations of a wave that occur in one second. It is typically measured in hertz (Hz) and represents how many times a wave repeats per second.

Wavelength: Wavelength is the distance between two consecutive corresponding points on a wave, such as from peak to peak or trough to trough. It is usually represented by the Greek letter lambda (λ) and is measured in units such as meters (m), nanometers (nm), or angstroms (Å), depending on the scale of the wave.

Amplitude: Amplitude represents the maximum displacement or distance from the equilibrium position of a wave. In other words, it measures the intensity or strength of a wave. For example, in a transverse wave, it refers to the distance from the baseline to the peak or trough of the wave. It can indicate the brightness or loudness of a wave, depending on the type of wave. Amplitude is typically measured in units such as meters (m) or volts (V).

20) Calculate the frequency of a photon (in Hz) with a wavelength of 877.4 nm.
A) 3.42x1014 Hz*
B) 0.000342 Hz
C) 2.92x10-15 Hz
D) 2.27x10-19 Hz

To calculate the frequency of a photon using its wavelength, you can use the following equation:

c = λ * ν

Where: c is the speed of light in a vacuum (3.00 x 108 m/s) λ is the wavelength of the photon in meters ν is the frequency of the photon in Hz

First, we need to convert the given wavelength of 877.4 nm to meters:

λ = 877.4 nm * \( \frac{1 m}{10^9 nm} \) = 8.77x10-7 m

Now we can rearrange the equation to solve for the frequency:

ν = c / λ = (3.00 x 108 m/s) / (8.77x10-7 m)

ν ≈ 3.42x1014 Hz

23) Determine the energy change associated with the transition from n=3 to n=5 in the hydrogen atom.
A) 1.55x10-19 J*
B) -1.55x10-19 J
C) -2.91x10-19 J
D) 2.91x10-19 J

The energy change associated with the transition between energy levels in a hydrogen atom can be calculated using the formula:

ΔE = - RH * \( (\frac{1}{n_{final}} - \frac{1}{n_{initial}}) \)

where RH is the Rydberg constant for hydrogen (2.18 × 10-18 J), nfinal is the final energy level, and ninitial is the initial energy level.

For this specific transition from n=3 to n=5, we can substitute the values into the formula:

ΔE = - RH * \( (\frac{1}{5} - \frac{1}{3}) \) = -(2.18 × 10-18 J) * \( (\frac{1}{5} - \frac{1}{3}) \) ≈ 1.55x10-19 J

25) What is it called when two waves of equal amplitude that are out of phase interact?
A) constructive interference
B) refraction
C) diffraction
D) destructive interference*
When two waves of equal amplitude that are in phase interact, it is called constructive interference. In constructive interference, the waves combine to produce a resulting wave with an increased amplitude. This occurs when the peaks of one wave align with the peaks of the other wave, or the troughs align with the troughs, leading to reinforcement and an overall amplification of the wave.

When two waves of equal amplitude that are out of phase interact, it is called destructive interference. In destructive interference, the waves combine in a way that their crests align with the troughs, resulting in a canceling out or reduction of the overall amplitude. This occurs when the peaks of one wave align with the troughs of the other wave, leading to a net decrease in amplitude or even complete cancellation of the wave.
