4) (SLO 1.1) The osmotic pressure of a solution formed by dissolving 24.1 mg of Aspirin (C9H8O4) (180.16 g/mol) in enough water to make 0.266 L of solution at 40°C is _____ atm.
A) 0.00165
B) 0.0129*
C) 12.9
D) 0.00344

Given: Mass of Aspirin = 24.1 mg x 1 g/1000 mg= 0.0241 g
Molar mass of Aspirin (C9H8O4) = 180.16 g/mol
Volume of solution (V) = 0.266 L

First, calculate the number of moles of Aspirin:

moles of Aspirin = 0.0236 g x (1mol Aspirin)/(180.16 g Aspirin) ≈ 0.000134 mol

Next, calculate the molarity (M) of the solution:

Molarity (M) = moles of solute / volume of solution Molarity (M) = 0.0000134 mol / 0.266 L
Molarity (M) ≈  0.000503 M

Now we can calculate the osmotic pressure:

π = MRT

where: M is the molarity of the solution R is the ideal gas constant (0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K)) T is the temperature in Kelvin (K)

Given: Temperature (T) = 40°C +273.15 = 313.15 K

π = (0.000503) * (0.08206 L·atm/(mol·K)) * (313.15 K) π ≈ 0.0129 atm

5) (SLO 1.3) The Henry’s law constant for oxygen gas in water at 20ºC is 14 x 10-4 M/atm. When the partial pressure of oxygen above a sample of water is 0.514 atm, the concentration of oxygen in the water is ______ M.
A) 0.00272
B) 0.000720*
C) 7.20
D) 367

Henry's law relates the concentration of a gas in a liquid to its partial pressure above the liquid. The equation for Henry's law is:

C = k * P

where: C is the concentration of the gas in the liquid (in Molarity, M) k is the Henry's law constant for the specific gas-solvent system (in M/atm) P is the partial pressure of the gas above the liquid (in atm)

Given: Henry's law constant (k) for oxygen gas in water = 14 x 10-4 M/atm
Partial pressure of oxygen (P) = 0.514 atm

Using Henry's law, we can calculate the concentration of nitrogen gas in the water (C):

C = (14 x 10-4 M/atm) * (0.514 atm) C ≈ 0.000720 M

6) (SLO 1.6) What is the change in boiling point of a solution of 32.6 g KCl (74.55 g/mol) in 784 g of water? Kb for water is 0.512°C/m.  Assume ideal behavior.
A) 0.571°C*
B) 0.000286°C
C) 21.3°C
D) 0.286°C

To calculate the change in boiling point of a solution, we can use the equation:

ΔTb = i x Kb x m

where: i = van't Hoff factor
ΔTb = change in boiling point
Kb = boiling point elevation constant (0.512 °C/m for water)
m = molality of the solution (moles of solute per kilogram of solvent)

First, we need to calculate the molality of the solution, which is the number of moles of solute per kilogram of solvent.

 32.6 g KCl x (1 mol KCl)/(74.55 g KCl) = 0.437 mol KCl

Step 2: Calculate the molality of the solution: Molality = moles of solute / mass of solvent (in kg) Mass of solvent (water) = 784 g x 1 kg/1000 g = 0.784 kg 

Molality =0.437 mol KCl mol / 0.784 kg ≈ 0.558  M

Step 3: van't Hoff factor
The van't Hoff factor for KCl will be estimated at 2 because  KCl contains 2 ions.

Step 3: Calculate the change in boiling point: ΔTb = i x Kb x m ΔTb = 2 x 0.512 °C/m * 0.558  M ≈ 0.571  M °C

11) (SLO 2.6) At 366 K a reaction is found to have a rate constant of  37300 M-1s-1, at 214 K the reaction is found to have a rate constant of 8.02 M-1s-1. What is the activation energy for this reaction.
A) 36.2 kJ/mol*
B) 15.7 kJ/mol
C) -36.2 kJ/mol
D) -15.7 kJ/mol

Starting with the Arrhenius equation:

\( ln \frac{k_2}{k_1} = -\frac{E_a}{R}(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}) \), multiplying each side by -R
\( -R x ln \frac{k_2}{k_1} = E_a x (\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}) \), dividing each side by \( (\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}) \)
\( \frac{-R x ln \frac{k_2}{k_1}}{(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}) } = E_a \), then plugging in 37300 M-1s-1 in for K1, 8.02 M-1s-1 in for K2,  0.0083145 kJ/mol-K for R,  366 K for T1, and 214 K for T2 gives:
Ea = (-0.0083145 kJ/mol-K x ln( 8.02 M-1s-1 / 37300 M-1s-1)) / (1/214 K - 1/366 K)
Ea = 36.2 kJ/mol

64) How much energy is required to heat 36.3 g H2O from a liquid at 57.6°C to a gas at 120°C?
ΔHvap = 40.7 kJ/mol
Cliquid = 4.184 \( \frac{J}{g°C} \) 
Cgas = 2.01 \( \frac{J}{g°C} \)  
Csolid = 2.09 \( \frac{J}{g°C} \) 
Tmelting = 0°C 
Tboiling = 100°C
A) 99.5 kJ
B) 89.9 kJ*
C) 1490 kJ
D) 7980 kJ

To calculate the energy required to heat water from a liquid at 57.6°C to a gas at 120°C, we need to consider the following steps:

  1. Heating the liquid water from its initial temperature to its boiling point.
  2. Vaporizing the liquid water at its boiling point to gas.
  3. Heating the water vapor from it's boiling point to 120°C.

Step 1: Heating the liquid water First, we calculate the energy required to heat the liquid water from 57.6°C to its boiling point at 100°C.

q1 = m x Cliquid x ΔT1

where: q1 = energy required (in joules) m = mass of water (in grams)
Cliquid = specific heat capacity of liquid water (in J/g-°C)
ΔT1 = change in temperature (final temperature - initial temperature)
Cgas = specific heat capacity of liquid water (in J/g-°C)

Given: m = 36.3 g
Cliquid = 4.184 J/g-°C
ΔT1 = 100°C - 57.6°C = 42.4°C

q1 = (36.3 g) x (4.184 J/g-°C) x (42.4°C)
q1 ≈ 6440 J x \( \frac{1 kJ}{1000 J} \) = 6.44 kJ

Step 2: Vaporizing the liquid water Next, we calculate the energy required to vaporize the liquid water at its boiling point.

q2 = n x ΔHvap

where: q2 = energy required (in joules)
n = number of moles of water
ΔHvap = enthalpy of vaporization (in J/mol)

To calculate the number of moles of water, we use the molar mass of water (H2O), which is approximately 18.02 g/mol.

mol = 36.3 x \( \frac{1 mol H_2O}{18.02 g H_2O} \) ≈ 2.01 mol

Given: ΔHvap = 40.7 kJ/mol 

q2 = (2.01 mol) * (40.7 kJ/mol) ≈ 82.0 kJ

Step 3: Heating the water vapor, we calculate the energy required to heat the water vapor from 100°C to 120°C.

q3 = m x Cliquid x ΔT2

where: q3 = energy required (in J) m = mass of water (in grams)
Cgas = specific heat capacity of liquid water (in J/g-°C)
ΔT1 = change in temperature (final temperature - initial temperature)

Given: m = 36.3 g
Cliquid = 2.01 J/g-°C
ΔT1 = 120°C - 100°C = 20.0°C

q3 = (36.3 g) x (2.01 J/g-°C) x (20.0°C)
q3 ≈ 1460 J x \( \frac{1 kJ}{1000 J} \) = 1.46 kJ

Total energy required: The total energy required is the sum of q1, q2 and q3.

Total energy = q1 + q2 + q3 ≈ 6.44 kJ + 82.0 kJ + 1.46 kJ

Total energy ≈ 89.9 kJ
