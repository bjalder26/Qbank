3) (SLO 1.1) The osmotic pressure of a solution formed by dissolving 36.2 mg of Aspirin (C9H8O4) (180.16 g/mol) in enough water to make 0.303 L of solution at 20°C is _____ atm.
A) 0.00483
B) 0.0160*
C) 0.00109
D) 16.0

Given: Mass of Aspirin = 36.2 mg x 1 g/1000 mg= 0.0362 g
Molar mass of Aspirin (C9H8O4) = 180.16 g/mol
Volume of solution (V) = 0.303 L

First, calculate the number of moles of Aspirin:

moles of Aspirin = 0.0236 g x (1mol Aspirin)/(180.16 g Aspirin) ≈ 0.000201 mol

Next, calculate the molarity (M) of the solution:

Molarity (M) = moles of solute / volume of solution Molarity (M) = 0.0000201 mol / 0.303 L
Molarity (M) ≈  0.000663 M

Now we can calculate the osmotic pressure:

π = MRT

where: M is the molarity of the solution R is the ideal gas constant (0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K)) T is the temperature in Kelvin (K)

Given: Temperature (T) = 20°C +273.15 = 293.15 K

π = (0.000663) * (0.08206 L·atm/(mol·K)) * (293.15 K) π ≈ 0.0160 atm

5) (SLO 1.3) The Henry’s law constant for hydrogen gas in water at 20ºC is 8.1 x 10-4 M/atm. When the partial pressure of hydrogen above a sample of water is 0.742 atm, the concentration of hydrogen in the water is ______ M.
A) 0.00109
B) 0.000601*
C) 6.01
D) 916

Henry's law relates the concentration of a gas in a liquid to its partial pressure above the liquid. The equation for Henry's law is:

C = k * P

where: C is the concentration of the gas in the liquid (in Molarity, M) k is the Henry's law constant for the specific gas-solvent system (in M/atm) P is the partial pressure of the gas above the liquid (in atm)

Given: Henry's law constant (k) for hydrogen gas in water = 8.1 x 10-4 M/atm
Partial pressure of hydrogen (P) = 0.742 atm

Using Henry's law, we can calculate the concentration of nitrogen gas in the water (C):

C = (8.1 x 10-4 M/atm) * (0.742 atm) C ≈ 0.000601 M

7) (SLO 1.6) What is the change in boiling point of a solution of 33.7 g K2SO4 (174.3 g/mol) in 739 g of water? Kb for water is 0.512°C/m.  Assume ideal behavior.
A) 23.3°C
B) 0.134°C
C) 0.402°C*
D) 0.000134°C

To calculate the change in boiling point of a solution, we can use the equation:

ΔTb = i x Kb x m

where: i = van't Hoff factor
ΔTb = change in boiling point
Kb = boiling point elevation constant (0.512 °C/m for water)
m = molality of the solution (moles of solute per kilogram of solvent)

First, we need to calculate the molality of the solution, which is the number of moles of solute per kilogram of solvent.

 33.7 g K2SO4 x (1 mol K2SO4)/(174.3 g K2SO4) = 0.193 mol K2SO4

Step 2: Calculate the molality of the solution: Molality = moles of solute / mass of solvent (in kg) Mass of solvent (water) = 739 g x 1 kg/1000 g = 0.739 kg 

Molality =0.193 mol K2SO4 mol / 0.739 kg ≈ 0.262  M

Step 3: van't Hoff factor
The van't Hoff factor for K2SO4 will be estimated at 3 because  K2SO4 contains 3 ions.

Step 3: Calculate the change in boiling point: ΔTb = i x Kb x m ΔTb = 3 x 0.512 °C/m * 0.262  M ≈ 0.402  M °C

13) (SLO 2.6) At 361 K a reaction is found to have a rate constant of  37000 (3 sf) M-1s-1, at 205 K the reaction is found to have a rate constant of 1.77 M-1s-1. What is the activation energy for this reaction.
A) 39.2 kJ/mol*
B) -17.0 kJ/mol
C) -39.2 kJ/mol
D) 17.0 kJ/mol

Starting with the Arrhenius equation:

\( ln \frac{k_2}{k_1} = -\frac{E_a}{R}(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}) \), multiplying each side by -R
\( -R x ln \frac{k_2}{k_1} = E_a x (\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}) \), dividing each side by \( (\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}) \)
\( \frac{-R x ln \frac{k_2}{k_1}}{(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}) } = E_a \), then plugging in 37000 (3 sf) M-1s-1 in for K1, 1.77 M-1s-1 in for K2,  0.0083145 kJ/mol-K for R,  361 K for T1, and 205 K for T2 gives:
Ea = (-0.0083145 kJ/mol-K x ln( 1.77 M-1s-1 / 37000 (3 sf) M-1s-1)) / (1/205 K - 1/361 K)
Ea = 39.2 kJ/mol

59) How much energy is required to heat 37.9 g H2O from a liquid at 52.1°C to a gas at 110°C?
ΔHvap = 40.7 kJ/mol
Cliquid = 4.184 \( \frac{J}{g°C} \) 
Cgas = 2.01 \( \frac{J}{g°C} \)  
Csolid = 2.09 \( \frac{J}{g°C} \) 
Tmelting = 0°C 
Tboiling = 100°C
A) 102 kJ
B) 1550 kJ
C) 94.0 kJ*
D) 8440 kJ

To calculate the energy required to heat water from a liquid at 52.1°C to a gas at 110°C, we need to consider the following steps:

  1. Heating the liquid water from its initial temperature to its boiling point.
  2. Vaporizing the liquid water at its boiling point to gas.
  3. Heating the water vapor from it's boiling point to 110°C.

Step 1: Heating the liquid water First, we calculate the energy required to heat the liquid water from 52.1°C to its boiling point at 100°C.

q1 = m x Cliquid x ΔT1

where: q1 = energy required (in joules) m = mass of water (in grams)
Cliquid = specific heat capacity of liquid water (in J/g-°C)
ΔT1 = change in temperature (final temperature - initial temperature)
Cgas = specific heat capacity of liquid water (in J/g-°C)

Given: m = 37.9 g
Cliquid = 4.184 J/g-°C
ΔT1 = 100°C - 52.1°C = 47.9°C

q1 = (37.9 g) x (4.184 J/g-°C) x (47.9°C)
q1 ≈ 7600 (3 sf) J x \( \frac{1 kJ}{1000 J} \) = 7.60 kJ

Step 2: Vaporizing the liquid water Next, we calculate the energy required to vaporize the liquid water at its boiling point.

q2 = n x ΔHvap

where: q2 = energy required (in joules)
n = number of moles of water
ΔHvap = enthalpy of vaporization (in J/mol)

To calculate the number of moles of water, we use the molar mass of water (H2O), which is approximately 18.02 g/mol.

mol = 37.9 x \( \frac{1 mol H_2O}{18.02 g H_2O} \) ≈ 2.10 mol

Given: ΔHvap = 40.7 kJ/mol 

q2 = (2.10 mol) * (40.7 kJ/mol) ≈ 85.6 kJ

Step 3: Heating the water vapor, we calculate the energy required to heat the water vapor from 100°C to 110°C.

q3 = m x Cliquid x ΔT2

where: q3 = energy required (in J) m = mass of water (in grams)
Cgas = specific heat capacity of liquid water (in J/g-°C)
ΔT1 = change in temperature (final temperature - initial temperature)

Given: m = 37.9 g
Cliquid = 2.01 J/g-°C
ΔT1 = 110°C - 100°C = 10.0°C

q3 = (37.9 g) x (2.01 J/g-°C) x (10.0°C)
q3 ≈ 762 J x \( \frac{1 kJ}{1000 J} \) = 0.762 kJ

Total energy required: The total energy required is the sum of q1, q2 and q3.

Total energy = q1 + q2 + q3 ≈ 7.60 kJ + 85.6 kJ + 0.762 kJ

Total energy ≈ 94.0 kJ
