1) What is it called when two waves of equal amplitude that are in phase interact?
A) diffraction
B) destructive interference
C) refraction
D) constructive interference*
When two waves of equal amplitude that are in phase interact, it is called constructive interference. In constructive interference, the waves combine to produce a resulting wave with an increased amplitude. This occurs when the peaks of one wave align with the peaks of the other wave, or the troughs align with the troughs, leading to reinforcement and an overall amplification of the wave.

When two waves of equal amplitude that are out of phase interact, it is called destructive interference. In destructive interference, the waves combine in a way that their crests align with the troughs, resulting in a canceling out or reduction of the overall amplitude. This occurs when the peaks of one wave align with the troughs of the other wave, leading to a net decrease in amplitude or even complete cancellation of the wave.

2) Which of the following sets of quantum numbers is possible?
A) (3, 4, -2, +\( \frac{1}{2} \))
B) (5, 4, +5, -\( \frac{1}{2} \))
C) (5, 3, -3, -\( \frac{1}{2} \))*
D) (4, 3, -3, -1)

For a set of quantum numbers to be valid, the following relationships must be satisfied:

  1. The principal quantum number (n) must be a positive integer (1, 2, 3, ...).
  2. The azimuthal quantum number (ℓ) must be an integer ranging from 0 to (n-1).
  3. The magnetic quantum number (mℓ) must be an integer ranging from -ℓ to +ℓ.
  4. The spin quantum number (ms) must be either +1/2 or -1/2.

Let's evaluate the given sets of quantum numbers:

(5, 3, -3, -\( \frac{1}{2} \))

    • n = 5 (valid as it is a positive integer).
    • ℓ = 3 (valid as it is within the range of 0 to (n-1)).
    • mℓ = -3 (valid as it is within the range of -ℓ to +ℓ).
    • ms = -\( \frac{1}{2} \) (valid as it is either +1/2 or -1/2).
    • Therefore, this set of quantum numbers is valid.
(3, 4, -2, +\( \frac{1}{2} \)) 
    • n = 3 (valid as it is a positive integer).
    • ℓ = 4 (invalid as it is not within the range of 0 to (n-1)).
    • mℓ = -2 (valid as it is within the range of -ℓ to +ℓ).
    • ms = +\( \frac{1}{2} \) (valid as it is either +1/2 or -1/2).
    • Therefore, this set of quantum numbers is not valid due to the value for ℓ.
(5, 4, +5, -\( \frac{1}{2} \)) 
    • n = 5 (valid as it is a positive integer).
    • ℓ = 4 (valid as it is within the range of 0 to (n-1)).
    • mℓ = +5 (invalid as it must be within the range of -ℓ to +ℓ).
    • ms = -\( \frac{1}{2} \)(valid as it is either +1/2 or -1/2).
    • Therefore, this set of quantum numbers is not valid due to the value of mℓ being outside the allowed range.
(4, 3, -3, - 1)
    • n = 4 (valid as it is a positive integer).
    • ℓ = 3 (valid as it is within the range of 0 to (n-1)).
    • mℓ = -3 (valid as it is within the range of -ℓ to +ℓ).
    • ms = -1 (invalid as it must be either +1/2 or -1/2).
    • Therefore, this set of quantum numbers is not valid due to the incorrect value for ms.

Based on the numerical relationships between the quantum numbers, only the set (5, 3, -3, -\( \frac{1}{2} \)) satisfies the required conditions and is therefore valid.

3) For n = 3, what are the possible subshells?
A) s
B) s and p
C) s‚ p‚ d and f
D) s‚ p and d*

For the principal quantum number (n) values of 1, 2, 3, and 4, the possible subshells are as follows:

n = 1: Only one subshell is possible, which is the s subshell.

n = 2: Two subshells are possible, which are the s and p subshells.

n = 3: Three subshells are possible, which are the s, p, and d subshells.

n = 4: Four subshells are possible, which are the s, p, d, and f subshells.

The subshells are named after the azimuthal quantum numbers (s, p, d, f) that represent the shape of the electron orbitals within each subshell.

4) Calculate the frequency of a photon (in Hz) with a wavelength of 187.6 nm.
A) 1.60x1015 Hz*
B) 0.00160 Hz
C) 6.25x10-16 Hz
D) 1.06x10-18 Hz

To calculate the frequency of a photon using its wavelength, you can use the following equation:

c = λ * ν

Where: c is the speed of light in a vacuum (3.00 x 108 m/s) λ is the wavelength of the photon in meters ν is the frequency of the photon in Hz

First, we need to convert the given wavelength of 187.6 nm to meters:

λ = 187.6 nm * \( \frac{1 m}{10^9 nm} \) = 1.88x10-7 m

Now we can rearrange the equation to solve for the frequency:

ν = c / λ = (3.00 x 108 m/s) / (1.88x10-7 m)

ν ≈ 1.60x1015 Hz

To calculate the wavelength of an electron, we can use the de Broglie wavelength equation:

λ = h / (m * v)

Where: λ is the wavelength h is Planck's constant (6.626 x 10-34 J·s) m is the mass of the electron v is the velocity of the electron

Let's substitute the given values into the equation:

λ = (6.626 x 10-34 J·s) / (9.11 × 10-31 Kg * 7.24 × 105 m/s)

λ ≈ 1.00x10-9 m

6) Which of the following happens when the frequency of a photon increases?
A) The speed increases
B) The wavelength increases
C) The wavelength stays the same
D) The wavelength decreases*

When the frequency and energy of a photon increase, the wavelength of the photon decreases. This relationship is a consequence of the wave-particle duality of light.

The relationship between wavelength (λ), frequency (ν), and speed of light (c) is given by the equation:

c = λν

As frequency increases, the wavelength decreases proportionally to maintain the constant speed of light. Similarly, energy (E) of a photon is directly proportional to its frequency (ν) according to the equation:

E = hν

where h is Planck's constant.

Therefore, when the frequency and energy of a photon increase, the wavelength of the photon decreases. This relationship holds true for electromagnetic waves across the entire electromagnetic spectrum.

7) What is the vertical height of a wave called?
A) frequency
B) amplitude*
C) wavediameter
D) wavelength

Frequency: Frequency refers to the number of complete cycles or oscillations of a wave that occur in one second. It is typically measured in hertz (Hz) and represents how many times a wave repeats per second.

Wavelength: Wavelength is the distance between two consecutive corresponding points on a wave, such as from peak to peak or trough to trough. It is usually represented by the Greek letter lambda (λ) and is measured in units such as meters (m), nanometers (nm), or angstroms (Å), depending on the scale of the wave.

Amplitude: Amplitude represents the maximum displacement or distance from the equilibrium position of a wave. In other words, it measures the intensity or strength of a wave. For example, in a transverse wave, it refers to the distance from the baseline to the peak or trough of the wave. It can indicate the brightness or loudness of a wave, depending on the type of wave. Amplitude is typically measured in units such as meters (m) or volts (V).

Wavediameter is a made-up term.

10) Describe the shape of a p orbital.
A) dumbbell shaped*
B) three lobes
C) spherical
D) four lobes

The shapes of the s, p, d, and f orbitals are as follows:

  1. s Orbital: The s orbital is spherical in shape and symmetrical around the nucleus. It has a single lobe without any nodal planes.

  2. p Orbitals: The p orbitals have a dumbbell shape. There are three p orbitals, designated as px, py, and pz. Each p orbital has two lobes on opposite sides of the nucleus and is oriented along the x, y, and z axes, respectively.

  3. d Orbitals: The d orbitals have complex shapes. There are five d orbitals, designated as dxy, dxz, dyz, dx^2-y^2, and dz^2. They have a combination of different lobes and nodal planes, resulting in various shapes.  

  4. f Orbitals: The f orbitals also have complex shapes. There are seven f orbitals, designated as fy3x^2-y^2, fxz^2, fz(x^2-y^2), fyz^2, fz(x^2-3y^2), fxyz, and fx(x^2-3y^2). These orbitals have even more intricate lobes, nodal surfaces, and orientations compared to the other orbitals.

It's important to note that these orbital shapes represent the probability distribution of finding an electron within a specific orbital, rather than the exact path of the electron.

12) Determine the frequency of light associated with the transition from n=5 to n=1  in the hydrogen atom.
A) 9.50x10-8 Hz
B) 2.63x1015 Hz
C) 2.09x10-18 Hz
D) 3.16x1015 Hz*

The energy (E) of a photon of light can be calculated from the frequency (v) with the following equation:
E = v * h, where h is Planck's constant (6.63 × 10-34 J·s) solving for frequency (v) gives us:
v = E/h

The energy (E) of a photon can be calculated using the following equation, where RH is the Rydberg constant for hydrogen (2.18*10-18 J), nfinal is the final energy level, ninitial is the initial energy level:
E = - RH * \( (\frac{1}{n_{final}} - \frac{1}{n_{initial}}) \) = -2.18*10-18 J * \( (\frac{1}{n_{final}} - \frac{1}{n_{initial}}) \)
If we substitute in -2.18*10-18 J * \( (\frac{1}{n_{final}} - \frac{1}{n_{initial}}) \) in for E in the equation v = E/h, then it gives the formula below:
v = -2.18*10-18 J * \( (\frac{1}{n_{final}} - \frac{1}{n_{initial}}) \)/h
For the transition from n=5 to n=1, we can substitute the values into the formula:

ν =   -2.18*10-18 J * \( (\frac{1}{n_{final}} - \frac{1}{n_{initial}}) \)/(6.63 × 10-34 J·s) ≈ 6.17 × 1014 Hz

Therefore, the frequency of light associated with the transition from n=5 to n=1 in the hydrogen atom is approximately 3.16x1015 Hz.

14) Predict the charge that nitrogen will take as an ion.
A) +3

The charges that elements can form as ions depend on their position in the periodic table and their tendency to gain or lose electrons to achieve a stable electron configuration.

Sodium (Na) tends to lose one electron to achieve a stable electron configuration, forming a +1 ion (Na+). Magnesium (Mg) tends to lose two electrons, forming a +2 ion (Mg2+). Aluminum (Al) also tends to lose three electrons, forming a +3 ion (Al3+). Nitrogen (N) tends to gain three electrons to achieve a stable electron configuration, forming a -3 ion (N3-). Oxygen (O) tends to gain two electrons, forming a -2 ion (O2-). Fluorine (F) tends to gain one electron, forming a -1 ion (F-).

So, the predicted charges for these elements as ions are: Sodium: +1 Magnesium: +2 Aluminum: +3 Nitrogen: -3 Oxygen: -2 Fluorine: -1

  • Ground State: The ground state refers to the lowest energy state of an atom or a molecule. In the ground state, electrons occupy the available orbitals starting from the lowest energy level (n = 1) and follow the rules of the Pauli Exclusion Principle and Hund's Rule.

  • Violation of the Pauli Exclusion Principle: The Pauli Exclusion Principle states that no two electrons in an atom can have the same set of quantum numbers. If there is a violation of this principle, it means that two or more electrons in an atom or a system have the same quantum numbers, which is not allowed.

  • Violation of Hund's Rule: Hund's Rule states that when electrons occupy degenerate orbitals (orbitals with the same energy), they tend to occupy separate orbitals with parallel spins before pairing up with opposite spins. A violation of Hund's Rule occurs when electrons in degenerate orbitals pair up with opposite spins before all the orbitals are singly occupied.

  • Excited State: An excited state refers to any energy state of an atom or a molecule that is higher in energy than the ground state. In an excited state, one or more electrons have absorbed energy and moved to higher energy levels or different orbitals. Electrons in excited states have configurations that deviate from the ground state electron configuration. Excited states are temporary and unstable, and electrons tend to return to lower energy levels or the ground state by releasing energy in the form of photons.

33) The statement "samples of a compound will always contain the same proportion of elements by mass" belongs to:
A) Dalton's Atomic Theory
B) The Law of Definite Proportions*
C) The Law of Conservation of Mass

Dalton's Atomic Theory: Matter is composed of small indestructible particles.

The Law of Conservation of Mass: In a chemical reaction, matter is neither created nor destroyed.

The Law of Multiple Proportions: If two elements form more than one compound, the ratios of the masses of the second element that combine with a fixed mass of the first element will always be ratios of small whole numbers.

The Law of Definite Proportions: Samples of a compound will always contain the same proportion of elements by mass.

34) What is the empirical formula for a sulfur oxide (compound composed of sulfur and oxygen) that is 66.7% sulfur by mass?
A) S2O
B) SO2
C) SO*
D) SO3
Since the sulfur oxide compound is is 66.7% sulfur by mass, it will be 100% - 66.7% = 33.3 % oxygen by mass.

Assuming a 100 g sample gives us:  66.7 g sulfur and 33.3 g oxygen.

Next convert those grams to moles.
66.7 g S x \( \frac{1 mol S}{32.06 g S} \) = 2.08 mol S
33.3 g O x \( \frac{1 mol O}{16.00 g O} \) = 2.08 mol O

Then divide by the lower of the two mol amounts, and get the lowest whole number ratio.
