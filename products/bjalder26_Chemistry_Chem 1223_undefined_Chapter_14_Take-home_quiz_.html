1) What generally happens with a reaction when the temperature is increased?
A) The rate constant (K) increases, causing the rate of the reaction to decrease
B) The rate constant (K) decreases, causing the rate of the reaction to increase
C) The rate constant (K) decreases, causing the rate of the reaction to decrease.
D) The rate constant (K) increases, causing the rate of the reaction to increase*
If you think about a generic rate equation such as Rate = k[A][B], anything that affects the rate, that isn't a change in concentration of A or B must cause a change in k.  Since an increase in temperature increases the rate of a reaction, it must be because it increases the value of k.

The effect of temperature on the equilibrium constant is shown in the Arrhenius equation.
k = \( Ae^{\frac{-E_a}{RT}} \)

3) A reaction is found to have an activation energy of 42.8 kJ/mol, and a frequency factor (A) of 6.82x1010 s-1.  What is the rate constant of the reaction at 375 K?
A) 6.24x1016 s-1
B) 7.45x104 s-1*
C) 5.96x104 s-1
D) 6.73x1010 s-1
K = \( Ae^{\frac{-E_a}{RT}} \)
Substituting in for 6.82x1010 s-1 A, 42.8 kJ/mol for Ea, 0.0083145 kJ/mol-K for R, and 375 K for T gives:
K = 6.82x1010 s-1 x e(-42.8 kJ/mol)/(0.0083145 kJ/mol-K x 375 K)
K = 7.45x104 s-1
*Ea and K need to both be in J or kJ

B) 0.247 s
C) 116 s

t1/2 = (2.42x10-2 M)/(2x2.81 Ms-1) = 0.00431 s

5) What is are the units for the k in the following rate law.

10) Given the following proposed mechanism, what is the rate law for the reaction?
A2 + B ➞ A2B (overall reaction)
step 1 A2 ➞ 2A fast
step 2 2A + B ➞ A2B slow
A) Rate = \( K[A_2]^{1/2}[B] \)
B) Rate = \( K[A_2]^2[B] \)
C) Rate = \( \frac{K[A_2]^2[B]}{2} \)
D) Rate = \( K[A_2][B] \)*
The rate law for the rate determining (slow) step (2A + B ➞ A2B) is:
rate = k2[A]2[B], however, we can't have an intermediate ([A]) in our final rate law.

Assume the first-fast reaction (A2 ➞ 2A) reaches equilibrium.
rateF = rateR
kF[A2] = kR[A]2 
Typically you would solve for the concentration of the intermediate ([A]), but in this case we can solve for [A]2
\( \frac{k_F}{k_R} \)[A2] = [A]2

Substitute in\( \frac{k_F}{k_R} \)[A2] for [A]2 in rate = k2[A]2[B] to get:
rate = k2\( \frac{k_F}{k_R} \)[A2][B], then combine the constants to get:
rate = K[A2][B]

14) The decomposition of cyclopropane to propene is a first-order reaction that has a rate constant of 6.31x10-4 s-1. If the initial concentration of cyclopropane is 2.55 M, what is the concentration of cyclopropane after 88.1 s?
A) 2.49 M
B) 2.97 M
C) 0.142 M
D) 2.41 M*
The integrate rate law for a first order reaction is \( ln\frac{[A]_t}{[A]_0} = -kt \)
Taking the inverse log of both sides gives:
\( \frac{[A]_t}{[A]_0} = e^{-kt} \), multiplying both sides by [A]0 gives:
\( [A]_t = [A]_0 x e^{-kt} \)
Plugging in 2.55 M for [A]0, 6.31x10-4 s-1 for K, and 88.1 s for t gives:
[A]t = 2.55 M x e(-6.31x10-4 s-1 x 88.1 s)
[A]t = 2.41 M

15) Identify the statement that is true.
A) The rate constant will always double if you double the temperature
B) Catalysts decrease the rate constant
C) Increasing the temperature generally increases the rate constant*
D) A catalyst is an intermediate in a reaction
If you look at a generic rate law (rate = k[A][B]), anything that affects the rate, besides changes in concentration are accounted for in the rate constant.  Since increasing temperature increases the rate of a reaction, it will do so by increasing the rate constant.  The same is true for adding a catalyst.
The affect temperature has on the rate constant is shown in the Arrhenius equation.  So is the effect of a catalyst, because it lowers the activation energy (Ea).
K = \( Ae^{\frac{-E_a}{RT}} \)

22) (SLO 2.6) At 345 K a reaction is found to have a rate constant of  34000 (3 sf) M-1s-1, at 241 K the reaction is found to have a rate constant of 3.40 M-1s-1. What is the activation energy for this reaction.
A) 61.2 kJ/mol*
B) -61.2 kJ/mol
C) 26.6 kJ/mol
D) -26.6 kJ/mol

Starting with the Arrhenius equation:

\( ln \frac{k_2}{k_1} = -\frac{E_a}{R}(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}) \), multiplying each side by -R
\( -R x ln \frac{k_2}{k_1} = E_a x (\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}) \), dividing each side by \( (\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}) \)
\( \frac{-R x ln \frac{k_2}{k_1}}{(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}) } = E_a \), then plugging in 34000 (3 sf) M-1s-1 in for K1, 3.40 M-1s-1 in for K2,  0.0083145 kJ/mol-K for R,  345 K for T1, and 241 K for T2 gives:
Ea = (-0.0083145 kJ/mol-K x ln( 3.40 M-1s-1 / 34000 (3 sf) M-1s-1)) / (1/241 K - 1/345 K)
Ea = 61.2 kJ/mol

23) (SLO 2.1) If the rate of appearance of H2 in the gas phase reaction is 0.106 M/s, then the rate of disappearance of HBr is _______  M/s
2 HBr (g) ➞ H2 (g) + Br2 (g)
A) 0.212 M/s*
B) 0.0530 M/s
C) 0.106 M/s
D) 9.43 M/s
Rate = \( -\frac{1}{2}\frac{Δ[HBr]}{Δt} \) = \( \frac{Δ[H_2]}{Δt} \) = \( \frac{Δ[Br_2]}{Δt} \)
If we substitute in 0.106 M/s for the change in concentration of H2, and solve for the rate of disappearance of HBr, then we'll get 0.212 M/s.
